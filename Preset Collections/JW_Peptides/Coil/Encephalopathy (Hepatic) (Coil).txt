Hepatic encephalopathy (HE) is a complex, potentially reversible neuropsychiatric syndrome arising from liver dysfunction, most commonly in the context of chronic liver disease such as cirrhosis, acute liver failure, or portosystemic shunting, leading to impaired detoxification of neurotoxic substances. The primary cause is hepatic insufficiency, where the liver fails to metabolize ammonia derived from gut bacterial urease activity on urea and amino acids, resulting in hyperammonemia that crosses the blood-brain barrier and induces cerebral edema, altered neurotransmission, and cognitive impairment. Additional contributing factors include gut dysbiosis promoting bacterial translocation and endotoxemia, systemic inflammation, oxidative stress from reactive oxygen species, manganese accumulation in basal ganglia causing extrapyramidal symptoms, and precipitating events like gastrointestinal bleeding, infections, electrolyte imbalances, or sedative use that exacerbate decompensation. Symptoms range from subtle cognitive changes in minimal HE (impaired attention, psychomotor slowing, sleep disturbances) to overt HE graded by West Haven criteria: grade 1 (euphoria, anxiety, shortened attention span), grade 2 (lethargy, disorientation, asterixis), grade 3 (somnolence, confusion), and grade 4 (coma). Pathophysiology involves multifaceted axes: ammonia-induced astrocytic swelling via glutamine accumulation (osmotic stress), neuroinflammation with microglial activation and cytokine release (IL-1{beta}, TNF-{alpha}), oxidative/nitrosative stress damaging neuronal mitochondria, altered GABAergic and glutamatergic neurotransmission, and disrupted gut-liver-brain axis with increased permeability allowing toxin influx. Globally, HE affects up to 40% of cirrhosis patients, significantly increasing mortality (up to 50% in severe cases), healthcare burden, and quality of life due to recurrent hospitalizations and caregiver strain. The proposed polypeptides address these mechanisms comprehensively. Real-world polypeptides include BPC157, which stabilizes gut integrity and counters vascular failure in portal hypertension; vasopressin, which manages portal hypertension by vasoconstriction; GLP-1, which modulates gut microbiota and metabolic aspects to reduce decompensation; cANP(4-23), which attenuates ammonia-induced oxidative stress via natriuretic peptide receptor activation; HIP/PAP, which promotes liver regeneration and anti-inflammatory effects potentially restoring astrocytic function; and beta-endorphin, which influences immune modulation through opioid pathways altered in HE. Designed polypeptides (flagged as D1 to D12) supplement coverage: D1 neutralizes ammonia by enhancing urea cycle enzymes; D2 suppresses neuroinflammation by inhibiting cytokine release; D3 mitigates oxidative stress through antioxidant motifs; D4 controls portal hypertension by promoting vascular remodeling; D5 restores astrocytic function by reducing swelling; D6 modulates immune responses to prevent systemic inflammation; D7 chelates manganese to avert neurotoxicity; D8 activates KATP channels for neuroprotection; D9 controls precipitants like infection via antimicrobial activity; D10 enhances CNS clearance of toxins; D11 addresses microbiome dysbiosis by selective bacterial inhibition; D12 supports overall gut-liver-brain axis integrity. Rationale is grounded in established pathways from peer-reviewed sources, with ammonia reduction central but expanded to multifactorial targets for synergy. Feasibility involves oral/IV delivery, short lengths for synthesis ease, low toxicity, and physicochemical optimization (cationic for membrane interaction, pI>7, 40% hydrophobicity) ensuring stability against proteases and bioavailability in hepatic impairment.
