Ichthyosis is a heterogeneous group of genetic skin disorders characterized by abnormal scaling and dryness of the skin, often resembling fish scales, hence the name derived from the Greek word 'ichthys' for fish. It encompasses over 20 subtypes, including ichthyosis vulgaris, X-linked ichthyosis, lamellar ichthyosis, epidermolytic ichthyosis, and Netherton syndrome, each with varying severity and associated features. Causes are predominantly genetic, involving mutations in genes critical for epidermal development and maintenance, such as FLG (filaggrin) in ichthyosis vulgaris leading to defective keratin filament aggregation, STS (steroid sulfatase) in X-linked ichthyosis causing cholesterol sulfate accumulation, TGM1 (transglutaminase 1) in autosomal recessive congenital ichthyosis impairing cornified envelope formation, ABCA12 in harlequin ichthyosis disrupting lipid transport, and SPINK5 in Netherton syndrome resulting in LEKTI deficiency and protease hyperactivity. Environmental factors like low humidity can exacerbate symptoms, but the primary etiology is hereditary, with autosomal dominant, recessive, or X-linked inheritance patterns. Symptoms typically manifest at birth or in early childhood and include widespread dry, rough skin with thick scales, pruritus (itching), erythema (redness), fissuring that can lead to pain and infection risk, ectropion (eyelid eversion) in severe cases, restricted joint movement due to taut skin, and secondary complications like overheating from impaired sweat glands or bacterial/fungal infections from barrier defects.
