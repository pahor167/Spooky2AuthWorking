Graves' disease is the most common cause of hyperthyroidism, an autoimmune disorder where thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin (TSI/TSAb) antibodies target the thyrotropin receptor (TSHR), mimicking TSH and causing uncontrolled thyroid hormone production. Genetic susceptibility (HLA-DR3, CTLA-4 polymorphisms) interacts with environmental triggers (stress, smoking, iodine excess, infections) to break immune tolerance, leading to B cell production of stimulating autoantibodies with T follicular helper cell support. Symptoms include weight loss despite increased appetite, palpitations, heat intolerance, tremor, anxiety, goiter, fatigue, muscle weakness, and menstrual irregularities; up to 30% develop Graves' orbitopathy (GO) with proptosis, periorbital edema, diplopia, pain, and potential vision loss from optic neuropathy; rare pretibial myxedema occurs from glycosaminoglycan deposition. Pathophysiology involves TSHR activation driving thyroid follicular hyperplasia, excessive T4/T3 synthesis/release, and systemic thyrotoxicosis with adrenergic sensitization; in GO, TSHR/IGF-1R crosstalk on orbital fibroblasts activates adipogenesis, hyaluronan overproduction, inflammation (cytokines IL-6, TNF-{alpha}), fibrosis, and extraocular muscle swelling in poorly vascularized retro-orbital space, perpetuating remodeling.

Current therapies (antithyroid drugs, radioiodine, surgery) manage symptoms but not autoimmunity, with high relapse, hypothyroidism risk, or incomplete GO resolution; emerging biologics (FcRn inhibitors, rituximab) reduce antibodies but are large proteins. The proposed short polypeptides offer targeted approaches with potential for better tolerability and delivery. Real polypeptide ATX-GD-59 inspired (investigational linear TSHR peptide from phase I trial) induces antigen-specific tolerance, reducing TSAb levels and improving thyroid function without generalized immunosuppression. Real disulfide-cyclic TSHR peptide (preclinical/patent-derived) modulates pathogenic antibodies, ameliorating hyperthyroidism and orbital changes in models via tolerance or antagonism. Designed head-to-tail cyclic amidated analog (literature-derived TSHR motif with cyclization and C-terminal amidation for protease resistance/enhanced stability) provides rational optimization for bioavailability and chronic exposure. These address core axes-autoantibody persistence, TSHR stimulation, and orbital inflammation-with favorable charge/solubility for injection, low predicted immunogenicity, and in silico stability; feasibility supported by analogous peptide trials/models, potential disease-modification complementing standards.
