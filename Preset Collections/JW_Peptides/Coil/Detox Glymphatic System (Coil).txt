Detox (Glymphatic System)

The glymphatic system is a perivascular network in the brain that facilitates the clearance of metabolic waste products, toxins, and misfolded proteins (such as amyloid-beta and tau) through convective flow of cerebrospinal fluid into the interstitial space, primarily driven by arterial pulsation and aquaporin-4 (AQP4) water channels on astrocytic endfeet. This system is most active during slow-wave sleep, when interstitial space expands and clearance efficiency increases substantially. Impaired glymphatic function, due to disrupted sleep, AQP4 mislocalization, or neuroinflammation-induced reactive astrogliosis, leads to toxin accumulation, contributing to cognitive impairment, neurodegeneration, and increased risk of conditions like Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, and traumatic brain injury sequelae.

The proposed polypeptides target three key therapeutic axes derived from established mechanisms: (1) promotion of slow-wave sleep to activate glymphatic flow, addressed by the real-world Delta sleep-inducing peptide (DSIP), a neuropeptide with documented sleep-inducing properties, and a designed DSIP-derived analog (N-acetylated and C-amidated with cationic extension for enhanced stability and solubility, based on rational modification of the native DSIP motif); (2) facilitation of AQP4-dependent fluid exchange, addressed by a designed de novo highly cationic polar peptide to support water transport and astrocytic membrane interactions; (3) mitigation of neuroinflammation to preserve perivascular integrity and AQP4 polarization, addressed by a designed repetitive KPV-motif peptide (derived from the anti-inflammatory core of alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone, with repetition for potency and termini protection for drug-likeness).
