"Preset_Notes=Brugada Syndrome for Coil

Brugada Syndrome is an inherited cardiac channelopathy characterized by a distinctive ST-segment elevation in the right precordial leads (V1-V3) and an increased risk of sudden cardiac death due to polymorphic ventricular tachycardia or ventricular fibrillation. It is primarily caused by loss-of-function mutations in the SCN5A gene, which encodes the alpha subunit of the cardiac sodium channel Na<sub>v</sub>1.5, resulting in reduced inward sodium current (I<sub>Na</sub>) during phase 0 of the action potential. This creates a transmural voltage gradient in the right ventricular epicardium, exacerbated by a prominent transient outward potassium current (I<sub>to</sub>), leading to premature repolarization and arrhythmogenesis. Additional genes (e.g., CACNA1C, SCN1B) affecting calcium or sodium channel function may contribute. Current management relies on implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs) for high-risk patients, with quinidine (a non-peptide I<sub>to</sub> blocker) used off-label in select cases. No approved peptide therapeutics exist, but emerging strategies include rescuing Na<sub>v</sub>1.5 trafficking defects and modulating ion currents. The proposed set includes one real-world investigational peptide derived from patent literature (Nav1.5-Trafficking-Peptide) designed to restore surface expression of mutant Na<sub>v</sub>1.5 channels, and two designed peptides: D2-ItoInhibitor, a conceptual I<sub>to</sub>-blocking peptide mimicking natural toxin effects without disulfide complexity, and D1-CaV1.2modulator, a short peptide hypothesized to support L-type calcium channel stability to counterbalance repolarization abnormalities. These candidates collectively address the three key pathophysiological axes: sodium current deficiency, excessive potassium efflux, and secondary calcium current insufficiency.
