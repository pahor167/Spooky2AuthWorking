Mechanistic Rationale 
Tuberculosis, caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (Mtb), involves complex host-pathogen interactions across multiple axes: (1) intracellular survival in macrophages via inhibition of phagolysosomal maturation; (2) biofilm and granuloma formation shielding bacteria; (3) immune evasion by downregulating MHC-II/HLA-DR and suppressing Th1/IFN-{gamma} responses; (4) induction of host cell necrosis over apoptosis to promote dissemination; (5) redox adaptation and resistance to oxidative/nitrosative stress in hypoxic granuloma cores; (6) modulation of autophagy and inflammasome activation (e.g., NLRP3 suppression); (7) ESX-1-mediated membrane damage and cytosolic access; (8) metabolic reprogramming of host macrophages toward lipid accumulation (foam-cell formation). The peptide set is designed to counter these via: 
- Direct mycobacterial membrane disruption (cationic amphipathic helices targeting mycolic acid-rich envelope) 
- Inhibition of ESAT-6/CFP-10 (ESX-1 secretion system) dimerization 
- Restoration of phagolysosomal acidification (V-ATPase recruitment enhancers) 
- HLA-DR transactivator (CIITA) stabilization peptides 
- NLRP3 inflammasome agonist scaffolds 
- Selective induction of mitochondrial apoptosis via BAX mimetics 
- Autophagy flux enhancers via Beclin-1/LC3 interface stabilizers 
- Foam-cell reversal via PPAR{gamma} antagonism and LXR{alpha} agonism motifs 
- Hypoxia-targeted delivery using nitroreductase-cleavable pro-motifs 
- Antioxidant catalytic mimetics (e.g., SOD/peroxiredoxin mimics) 

All peptides are de novo, chemically feasible, and collectively cover the 8 central pathophysiological axes. Each incorporates noncanonical residues or PTMs for protease resistance, membrane penetration, or subcellular targeting. Terminal modifications enhance stability, half-life, or organelle specificity.
