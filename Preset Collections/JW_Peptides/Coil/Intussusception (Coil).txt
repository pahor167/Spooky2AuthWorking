Intussusception is a serious medical condition primarily affecting infants and young children, though it can occur in adults as well. It involves the telescoping of one segment of the intestine into an adjacent segment, leading to bowel obstruction, reduced blood flow, and potential tissue death if not addressed promptly. Causes in children are often idiopathic, potentially triggered by viral infections leading to lymphoid hyperplasia in the intestinal wall, which acts as a lead point for the invagination. In adults, it is more commonly secondary to underlying pathologies such as tumors, polyps, adhesions from prior surgeries, or inflammatory conditions like Crohn's disease. Symptoms typically include sudden, severe abdominal pain that comes and goes in waves, vomiting (often bilious), bloody stools resembling currant jelly due to mucosal sloughing and bleeding, lethargy, a palpable sausage-shaped mass in the abdomen, and signs of shock in severe cases. Pathophysiologically, the condition arises from disrupted peristalsis where a proximal bowel segment (intussusceptum) invaginates into the distal segment (intussuscipiens), causing venous congestion, edema, arterial compromise, ischemia, and possible perforation or peritonitis if untreated. Risk factors include recent viral illnesses, Meckel's diverticulum, or, in rare cases, medications affecting gut motility like glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists. Early diagnosis via ultrasound or contrast enema is crucial, with treatment focusing on non-surgical reduction in children or surgical intervention in complicated or adult cases.
