Scurvy is a nutritional deficiency disease caused by prolonged lack of vitamin C (ascorbic acid) in the diet, leading to a range of debilitating symptoms and potentially fatal complications if left untreated. The primary cause is insufficient intake of fresh fruits and vegetables, which are the main dietary sources of vitamin C, though it can also arise from malabsorption issues such as those seen in gastrointestinal disorders, alcoholism, or restrictive diets. Historically prevalent among sailors, explorers, and populations during famines, scurvy manifests initially with nonspecific symptoms like fatigue, malaise, muscle weakness, and irritability. As the deficiency progresses, more characteristic signs emerge, including swollen and bleeding gums (gingivitis), easy bruising (ecchymoses), pinpoint hemorrhages around hair follicles (perifollicular petechiae), poor wound healing, joint pain and swelling due to hemarthrosis, anemia from blood loss or impaired iron absorption, and brittle hair that coils like a corkscrew. In advanced stages, patients may experience internal bleeding, infections, convulsions, heart failure, and death. Pathophysiologically, vitamin C serves as an essential cofactor for enzymes like prolyl and lysyl hydroxylases, which are critical for stabilizing the triple-helix structure of collagen through hydroxylation of proline and lysine residues. Without this, collagen becomes unstable, resulting in weakened blood vessels, skin, bones, and other connective tissues. Additionally, vitamin C plays roles in carnitine biosynthesis for energy metabolism, catecholamine and neurotransmitter synthesis (e.g., dopamine, norepinephrine), antioxidant defense against reactive oxygen species, immune cell function (enhancing neutrophil motility and phagocytosis), and hormone production via peptide amidation. Deficiency disrupts these processes, leading to metabolic imbalances like hypoglycemia, cognitive impairments, and dysregulated stress responses via the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Bone health suffers from impaired osteoid formation, causing subperiosteal hemorrhages and fractures, while vascular fragility promotes hemorrhages and edema.
