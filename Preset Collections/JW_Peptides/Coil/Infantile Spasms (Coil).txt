Infantile spasms, also known as West syndrome or infantile epileptic spasms syndrome (IESS), is a rare but severe form of epilepsy that typically manifests in infants between 3 and 12 months of age, with peak onset around 4-7 months. It is characterized by clusters of sudden, brief muscle contractions or jerks (spasms), often involving the head, trunk, and limbs in flexion, extension, or mixed patterns. These spasms occur in series, lasting seconds each, with clusters comprising 5-30 spasms and happening multiple times daily, particularly upon waking or during transitions between sleep stages. Accompanying symptoms include developmental stagnation or regression, such as loss of previously acquired skills like smiling, rolling over, or babbling, and potential cognitive impairments if untreated. The condition is often associated with hypsarrhythmia, a chaotic, high-amplitude EEG pattern reflecting widespread brain dysfunction. Causes are diverse: structural (e.g., brain malformations, perinatal hypoxia-ischemia, tuberous sclerosis complex), genetic (mutations in ARX, CDKL5, STXBP1, SCN2A), metabolic (e.g., pyridoxine deficiency), infectious (e.g., congenital infections), or idiopathic/cryptogenic in about 20-30% of cases. Pathophysiology is multifactorial, involving a 'final common pathway' where early brain insults or genetic factors disrupt neuronal maturation, leading to hyperexcitability. Key elements include overactivation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis with excessive corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) promoting seizures in immature brain circuits; imbalance in excitatory (glutamatergic) and inhibitory (GABAergic) neurotransmission, with delayed GABA switch from excitatory to inhibitory; altered growth factor signaling impairing interneuron development and synaptic plasticity; ion channel dysfunction affecting membrane stability; and neuroinflammatory responses exacerbating neuronal damage.
