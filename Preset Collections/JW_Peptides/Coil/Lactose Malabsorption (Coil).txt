Lactose malabsorption is a prevalent gastrointestinal disorder where the small intestine fails to adequately hydrolyze lactose, the disaccharide sugar found in milk and dairy products, due to diminished activity or absence of the brush border enzyme lactase (lactase-phlorizin hydrolase). This leads to undigested lactose reaching the colon, where it undergoes bacterial fermentation, producing short-chain fatty acids, hydrogen, methane, and carbon dioxide, resulting in osmotic diarrhea and gas accumulation. Primary causes include genetic downregulation of lactase expression post-weaning (non-persistence), common in approximately 70% of the global adult population, with higher incidence in Asian, African, Hispanic, and Native American ethnic groups. Secondary forms arise from intestinal damage due to infections (e.g., rotavirus, giardiasis), inflammatory conditions (e.g., Crohn's disease, celiac disease), radiation therapy, or medications affecting mucosal integrity. Congenital lactase deficiency is rare, inherited autosomal recessively, manifesting in infancy. Symptoms manifest 30 minutes to 2 hours post-lactose ingestion and encompass abdominal cramps, bloating, flatulence, watery diarrhea, nausea, borborygmi, and occasionally vomiting or systemic effects like headache and fatigue if severe. Pathophysiology involves osmotic pull drawing water into the bowel lumen, colonic distension from gas, and potential low-grade inflammation from fermentation byproducts, exacerbating visceral hypersensitivity in overlapping conditions like irritable bowel syndrome. Diagnosis typically involves hydrogen breath testing, lactose tolerance tests, or genetic screening for lactase persistence alleles, with management historically focused on dietary avoidance, though this risks nutritional deficiencies in calcium, vitamin D, and protein.
