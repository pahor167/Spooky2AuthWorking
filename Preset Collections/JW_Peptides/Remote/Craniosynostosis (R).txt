Craniosynostosis is a congenital disorder involving the premature fusion of one or more cranial sutures, the fibrous joints between the bones of the skull, which normally remain open during infancy and childhood to allow for brain growth and skull expansion. This condition affects approximately 1 in 2,000 to 2,500 live births and can be isolated (nonsyndromic) or part of a syndrome (syndromic), such as Crouzon, Apert, Pfeiffer, or Saethre-Chotzen syndromes. The primary causes are genetic mutations, most commonly in genes encoding fibroblast growth factor receptors (FGFR1, FGFR2, FGFR3), TWIST1, MSX2, and others involved in cranial bone development, leading to dysregulated signaling pathways that promote excessive osteoblast differentiation and bone formation at the sutures. Environmental factors like maternal smoking or advanced paternal age may also contribute. Symptoms vary depending on the affected suture(s) and include abnormal head shape (e.g., scaphocephaly from sagittal suture fusion, brachycephaly from coronal fusion, trigonocephaly from metopic fusion), facial asymmetry, bulging fontanelles, increased intracranial pressure manifesting as headaches, vomiting, irritability, developmental delays, visual impairments, seizures, and in severe cases, hydrocephalus or cognitive deficits due to restricted brain growth. Pathophysiology centers on disrupted suture biology: normally, sutures maintain patency through a balance of proliferation and differentiation in mesenchymal stem cells, osteoprogenitors, and osteoblasts; mutations enhance FGFR signaling, activating downstream ERK/MAPK, BMP, and TGF-{beta} pathways, which accelerate osteogenesis, reduce apoptosis, and alter mechanotransduction via ciliary signaling and stiffness responses, leading to premature ossification and suture closure. Syndromic forms may involve additional systemic issues like limb abnormalities or hearing loss. Current management is primarily surgical, such as cranial vault remodeling or endoscopic strip craniectomy, to relieve pressure and correct shape, often with helmet therapy; however, complications include blood loss, infection, and recurrence. No curative pharmacologic therapy exists, but emerging research targets molecular pathways for prevention or delay of fusion. The proposed polypeptides address craniosynostosis by targeting key therapeutic axes: real-world CNP variants inhibit FGFR signaling at the RAF1 level via NPR2 activation, reducing ERK activity and promoting suture patency, as demonstrated in Crouzon mouse models with improved skull morphology and feasibility via subcutaneous injection, considering solubility and half-life enhancements like PEGylation. Designed polypeptides (D1 for TGF-{beta} modulation, D2 for BMP regulation, D3 for ERK inhibition, D4 for TWIST1 enhancement) conceptually mimic antagonists or activators, with physicochemical properties optimized for stability (e.g., cyclic structures if needed, amidation), bioavailability, and targeted delivery to cranial sutures, supported by peptide therapeutics in bone disorders.
