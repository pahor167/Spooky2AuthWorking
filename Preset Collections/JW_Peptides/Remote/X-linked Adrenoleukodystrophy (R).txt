X-linked adrenoleukodystrophy (ALD) is a rare peroxisomal genetic disorder caused by mutations in the ABCD1 gene on the X chromosome, encoding the adrenoleukodystrophy protein (ALDP), a peroxisomal membrane transporter responsible for importing very long-chain fatty acids (VLCFA) into peroxisomes for {beta}-oxidation. Defective ALDP leads to toxic accumulation of VLCFA (particularly C26:0 and C24:0) in plasma and tissues, most critically affecting the central nervous system white matter, spinal cord, and adrenal cortex. Clinical phenotypes vary widely even within families: the childhood cerebral form (onset 4-10 years) involves rapid progressive inflammatory demyelination with behavioral changes, cognitive decline, vision/hearing loss, seizures, and vegetative state/death within years if untreated; adrenomyeloneuropathy (AMN, adult-onset) features slowly progressive spastic paraparesis, sensory loss, bladder dysfunction, and adrenal insufficiency; Addison-only form presents primary adrenal failure; heterozygous females may develop milder AMN-like symptoms later in life. Pathophysiology encompasses direct VLCFA-induced membrane perturbation, endoplasmic reticulum stress, oxidative damage from excess ROS, mitochondrial dysfunction, microglial activation with proinflammatory cytokine release, blood-brain barrier disruption, oligodendrocyte apoptosis, and axon loss, culminating in inflammatory cerebral demyelination resembling autoimmune processes while adrenal involvement causes cortisol/aldosterone deficiency and potential life-threatening crises.
