Eastern Equine Encephalitis (EEE) is a rare but potentially fatal mosquito-borne viral disease caused by the Eastern equine encephalitis virus (EEEV), an alphavirus in the Togaviridae family. The virus is endemic in North America, primarily along the Atlantic and Gulf coasts, with occasional outbreaks. It maintains an enzootic cycle between wild birds and Culiseta melanura mosquitoes in freshwater hardwood swamps, with bridge vectors (e.g., Aedes or Coquillettidia species) transmitting to humans and horses as dead-end hosts. Human cases are sporadic, with an average of 7-10 reported annually in the US, but carry high severity.

Symptoms range from asymptomatic or mild febrile illness (fever, headache, myalgia) to severe neuroinvasive disease (encephalitis or meningitis), with sudden onset high fever, vomiting, altered mental status, seizures, and coma. Case fatality rate is approximately 30-50%, and survivors often suffer permanent neurologic deficits such as cognitive impairment, paralysis, or seizures.

Pathophysiology involves initial viral replication at the bite site and lymph nodes, viremia, crossing of the blood-brain barrier, direct neuronal infection, massive inflammatory response with cytokine storm, vasogenic and cytotoxic edema, neuronal destruction, and disruption of CNS homeostasis.

There is no approved specific antiviral treatment or human vaccine; management is entirely supportive (ventilation, seizure control, edema reduction). Experimental approaches in related alphaviruses suggest potential for antivirals, but none are established for EEE.
