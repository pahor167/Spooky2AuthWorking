Crepitations in the lungs, also known as crackles or rales, represent abnormal adventitious respiratory sounds detected during auscultation of the chest, characterized by discontinuous, popping, or bubbling noises that occur primarily during inspiration but can sometimes be heard during expiration as well. These sounds arise from the sudden equalization of pressure in small airways or alveoli that have been collapsed or filled with fluid, leading to the rapid opening of these structures. Common causes include acute infections such as bacterial or viral pneumonia, where inflammation and exudate accumulation in the alveoli disrupt normal air flow; congestive heart failure, which results in pulmonary edema due to increased hydrostatic pressure in the pulmonary capillaries forcing fluid into the interstitial and alveolar spaces; chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), involving airway obstruction, mucus hypersecretion, and emphysema that contribute to uneven ventilation; interstitial lung diseases (ILD) like idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF), where progressive scarring and stiffening of lung tissue lead to restricted lung expansion; bronchitis, characterized by inflammation of the bronchial tubes with excessive mucus production; atelectasis from collapsed lung segments; and other conditions such as bronchiectasis, aspiration, or radiation-induced lung injury. Symptoms associated with lung crepitations often include shortness of breath (dyspnea), especially on exertion or when lying flat (orthopnea), persistent cough that may be productive of sputum or blood-tinged, chest pain or tightness, fatigue, cyanosis indicating hypoxemia, and in severe cases, respiratory distress requiring supplemental oxygen. The pathophysiology involves multiple overlapping mechanisms: infection triggers an immune response with cytokine release leading to alveolar inflammation and fluid leakage; fluid overload in edema impairs gas exchange by increasing the diffusion barrier; fibrosis alters lung architecture, reducing compliance and causing ventilation-perfusion mismatches; airway obstruction and mucus plugging exacerbate atelectasis and uneven airflow; and overall, these lead to hypoxemia, hypercapnia, and increased work of breathing, potentially progressing to respiratory failure if untreated.
