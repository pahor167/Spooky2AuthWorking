Echinococcosis is a neglected zoonotic parasitic disease caused by infection with larval stages (metacestodes) of Echinococcus tapeworms, predominantly Echinococcus granulosus sensu lato (causing cystic echinococcosis, CE) and Echinococcus multilocularis (causing alveolar echinococcosis, AE). Transmission occurs via fecal-oral route through ingestion of eggs from definitive hosts (canids), with humans as accidental intermediate hosts. Oncospheres hatch in the intestine, penetrate the wall, and migrate via blood/lymph to organs (primarily liver ~70%, lungs ~20%, occasionally brain, bone, or other sites), developing into fluid-filled cysts (CE) or infiltrative multivesicular lesions (AE). Symptoms arise from mechanical pressure, including abdominal/lung pain, jaundice, biliary obstruction, or cough/hemoptysis; cyst rupture risks anaphylaxis and secondary dissemination. AE behaves like a malignant tumor with endless peripheral proliferation, leading to organ failure if untreated. Pathophysiology features parasite immune evasion via laminated layer, host Th2-biased response in CE (tolerance), or chronic inflammation in AE, with fibrosis and angiogenesis supporting growth.

Standard management includes surgery (cyst removal), percutaneous PAIR (puncture-aspiration-injection-reaspiration with scolicidal agents), long-term benzimidazoles (albendazole preferred) targeting parasite microtubules, or watch-and-wait for inactive CE cysts. Prevention relies on deworming dogs and EG95 vaccination in livestock (recombinant protein, not human-use peptide).

Incorporation of non-standard residues and modifications aims to improve bioavailability, resistance to proteolysis, and targeted efficacy against parasite viability/growth while minimizing host toxicity.
