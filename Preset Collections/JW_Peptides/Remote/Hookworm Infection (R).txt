Hookworm infection, also known as hookworm disease, is a widespread parasitic infestation primarily caused by the soil-transmitted helminths Necator americanus and Ancylostoma duodenale, with occasional involvement of Ancylostoma ceylanicum. Transmission occurs when infective larvae in contaminated soil penetrate the skin, often through bare feet, leading to a condition called 'ground itch' characterized by localized pruritus and erythema. The larvae then migrate via the bloodstream to the lungs, where they cause respiratory symptoms such as cough, wheezing, and hemoptysis as they ascend the bronchial tree and are swallowed into the gastrointestinal tract. In the small intestine, they mature into adult worms that attach to the mucosal wall using hook-like teeth or cutting plates, feeding on host blood and causing chronic blood loss. This results in iron-deficiency anemia, hypoproteinemia, and malnutrition, particularly in endemic areas with poor sanitation and hygiene. Symptoms vary by infection intensity: light infections may be asymptomatic, while heavy burdens lead to fatigue, weakness, pallor, abdominal pain, diarrhea, weight loss, and in children, stunted growth, cognitive impairments, and developmental delays. Pathophysiology involves mechanical trauma to the intestinal mucosa, anticoagulant secretion by worms to facilitate blood feeding, immune evasion through modulation of host responses (e.g., induction of Th2-biased immunity with eosinophilia and elevated IgE), and secondary complications like bacterial superinfections or heart failure in severe anemia. Chronic infections exacerbate poverty cycles in tropical and subtropical regions, affecting over 400 million people globally as of recent estimates.
