Neuromyelitis Optica (NMO), also known as Devic’s Disease, is a rare autoimmune inflammatory disorder primarily affecting the optic nerves and spinal cord, characterized by severe demyelination and axonal damage. It is driven by pathogenic autoantibodies, predominantly against aquaporin-4 (AQP4), a water channel protein expressed on astrocytes, leading to astrocytopathy. The causes include genetic predisposition, environmental triggers such as infections, and immune dysregulation, resulting in complement activation, cytokine release, and infiltration of inflammatory cells like neutrophils and eosinophils into the central nervous system. Symptoms typically manifest as acute attacks of optic neuritis causing vision loss, transverse myelitis leading to limb weakness, sensory disturbances, and bladder dysfunction, often with severe pain and potential respiratory involvement in extensive spinal lesions. The pathophysiology involves AQP4-IgG binding to astrocytes, triggering complement-dependent cytotoxicity, antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity, and disruption of the blood-brain barrier, which exacerbates inflammation and secondary demyelination. Relapsing forms predominate, with each attack contributing to cumulative neurological disability, differing from multiple sclerosis by its more aggressive course and preferential involvement of opticospinal regions.
