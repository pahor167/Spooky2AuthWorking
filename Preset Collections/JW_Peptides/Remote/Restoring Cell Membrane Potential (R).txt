Cell membrane potential, commonly referred to as the resting membrane potential, represents the electrical voltage difference across the plasma membrane of cells, typically maintained between -40 mV and -90 mV in various cell types, with neurons around -70 mV and cardiac myocytes around -90 mV. This potential is crucial for fundamental cellular processes, including nerve impulse transmission, muscle contraction, nutrient uptake, and overall cellular homeostasis. Disruptions to cell membrane potential can arise from multiple causes, such as physical trauma causing membrane pores or tears, oxidative stress from reactive oxygen species leading to lipid peroxidation and altered membrane permeability, genetic mutations affecting ion channel function (e.g., channelopathies like long QT syndrome), toxin exposure blocking channels or pumps, energy depletion impairing ATP-dependent ion pumps, inflammatory responses altering channel expression, or pathological states like ischemia, hypoxia, neurodegenerative diseases (e.g., Alzheimer's, Parkinson's), cardiac arrhythmias, or cancer where bioelectric signaling is dysregulated. Symptoms vary by cell type: in neurons, depolarization may cause hyperexcitability leading to seizures or pain, while hyperpolarization results in reduced signaling and neuropathy; in cardiac cells, it manifests as irregular heartbeats or fibrillation; in skeletal muscle, weakness, cramps, or paralysis; and systemically, it can lead to cell swelling, apoptosis, or necrosis if prolonged. The pathophysiology involves the breakdown of ion concentration gradients-high intracellular K+ and low Na+-maintained by the Na+/K+ ATPase pump and leak channels. When disrupted, passive ion flux alters the Nernst equilibrium, shifting the potential away from rest, impairing action potential generation, and triggering downstream cascades like calcium overload, mitochondrial dysfunction, and inflammatory signaling, ultimately compromising cell viability.
