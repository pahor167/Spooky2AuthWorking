Lennox-Gastaut Syndrome (LGS) is a severe, rare developmental and epileptic encephalopathy that typically emerges in childhood, often between ages 2 and 6, though onset can range from infancy to late childhood. It affects approximately 1-4% of childhood epilepsies and is characterized by a triad of features: multiple types of intractable seizures (most commonly tonic, atonic/drop attacks, and atypical absences, but also myoclonic, generalized tonic-clonic, and focal seizures); cognitive impairment or regression with behavioral disturbances; and distinctive electroencephalographic abnormalities, including slow spike-and-wave discharges (1.5-2.5 Hz) during wakefulness and bursts of paroxysmal fast activity (10-20 Hz) during sleep. Symptoms include frequent drop attacks causing falls and injuries, developmental stagnation or loss of milestones, intellectual disability (often moderate to severe), and psychiatric comorbidities like autism spectrum traits or hyperactivity. Prognosis is generally poor, with lifelong refractory seizures in most patients, high risk of sudden unexpected death in epilepsy (SUDEP), and significant dependence in adulthood.

The pathophysiology is heterogeneous and multifactorial, often evolving from prior epileptic encephalopathies (e.g., infantile epileptic spasms syndrome/West syndrome in 10-30%) or underlying brain insults. Identified etiologies in 60-75% of cases include structural abnormalities (cortical dysplasias, tuberous sclerosis complex), genetic mutations (affecting ion channels like SCN2A, synaptic proteins like STXBP1, or chromatin regulators like CHD2), metabolic disorders, or perinatal injuries; the remainder are cryptogenic. Core mechanisms involve disrupted thalamocortical network dynamics leading to hyperexcitability, imbalance between excitatory (glutamatergic) and inhibitory (GABAergic) neurotransmission, aberrant signaling pathways (e.g., hyperactive mTOR in TSC-related cases), and secondary network epileptogenesis. Chronic seizures exacerbate neuronal damage, inflammation, and cognitive decline in a vicious cycle.

Current management relies on antiseizure medications (e.g., valproate, clobazam, lamotrigine, rufinamide, cannabidiol), dietary therapies (ketogenic diet), neuromodulation (vagus nerve stimulation, deep brain stimulation), or resective surgery in rare focal cases, but these are largely symptomatic with limited impact on cognition or long-term seizure freedom.
