Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (HH) is a disorder where the testes fail to produce adequate testosterone and sperm due to insufficient stimulation by pituitary gonadotropins (luteinizing hormone [LH] and follicle-stimulating hormone [FSH]), resulting from deficient gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) secretion from the hypothalamus or impaired pituitary function. Causes can be congenital, such as genetic mutations affecting the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis (e.g., mutations in genes like KISS1, GNRHR, or KAL1), or acquired, including pituitary tumors, head trauma, infiltrative diseases like hemochromatosis, chronic illnesses, opioid use, or functional factors like extreme stress, malnutrition, or obesity leading to suppressed GnRH pulsatility. Symptoms typically include delayed or absent puberty, infertility, erectile dysfunction, decreased libido, fatigue, reduced muscle mass and strength, osteoporosis, gynecomastia, and sparse body hair. Pathophysiologically, the disrupted HPG axis leads to low serum testosterone (<300 ng/dL), low or inappropriately normal LH/FSH levels, and impaired spermatogenesis, often with small testes (<15 mL). Diagnosis involves hormonal assays, MRI for structural abnormalities, and genetic testing if indicated. Untreated, it can lead to long-term complications like cardiovascular disease and metabolic syndrome.
