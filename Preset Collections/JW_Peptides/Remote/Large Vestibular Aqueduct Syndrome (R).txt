Large Vestibular Aqueduct Syndrome (LVAS), also known as Enlarged Vestibular Aqueduct Syndrome, is a rare congenital malformation of the inner ear characterized by an abnormally large vestibular aqueduct, the bony canal that contains the endolymphatic duct and sac. This enlargement disrupts normal fluid homeostasis in the inner ear, leading to sensorineural hearing loss that is often progressive, fluctuating, or sudden in onset. The condition is frequently associated with mutations in the SLC26A4 gene, which encodes the pendrin protein responsible for anion exchange and pH regulation in the endolymph. Causes include genetic factors, with autosomal recessive inheritance in many cases, and it can occur in isolation or as part of Pendred syndrome, which also involves thyroid dysfunction. Symptoms typically manifest in childhood and include bilateral or unilateral hearing loss, tinnitus, vertigo, imbalance, and increased susceptibility to hearing deterioration following minor head trauma, barotrauma (e.g., from flying or scuba diving), or even strenuous exercise. Pathophysiology involves abnormal pressure transmission from the cerebrospinal fluid to the endolymphatic system, causing mechanical stress on cochlear hair cells and spiral ganglion neurons, leading to apoptosis, oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and inflammation. This can result in endolymphatic hydrops similar to Meniere's disease, contributing to vestibular symptoms. Without intervention, hearing loss can progress to profound levels, impacting speech development, social interaction, and quality of life. Current management is supportive, focusing on avoiding triggers, using hearing aids, or cochlear implantation for severe cases, but no curative treatments exist.
