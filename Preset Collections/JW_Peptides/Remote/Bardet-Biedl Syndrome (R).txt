Bardet-Biedl Syndrome (BBS) is a rare autosomal recessive ciliopathy caused by mutations in over 20 genes (BBS1-BBS26 identified to date) encoding proteins essential for primary cilium biogenesis, structure, and function, particularly the BBSome complex involved in ciliary trafficking. The condition manifests with a multisystem phenotype including progressive rod-cone retinal dystrophy leading to severe vision impairment or blindness, early-onset severe obesity driven by intractable hyperphagia, postaxial polydactyly, renal anomalies (such as cystic dysplasia progressing to end-stage kidney disease), hypogonadotropic hypogonadism, cognitive and developmental delays, and increased risk of metabolic complications like type 2 diabetes and hypertension. Pathophysiology centers on defective primary cilia, impairing critical signaling pathways (e.g., leptin receptor trafficking causing leptin resistance, disrupted melanocortin signaling in the hypothalamus, aberrant Hedgehog and Wnt pathways during development, and impaired intraflagellar transport affecting photoreceptor outer segments and renal tubular function).

Another designed polypeptide (flagged as designed) conceptually mimics GLP-1 receptor activation based on emerging evidence of GLP-1 agonists' benefits in BBS models and patients, promoting satiety, insulin sensitivity, and additional weight reduction. A further designed polypeptide (flagged as designed) is a polybasic cell-penetrating sequence conceptually aimed at improving intracellular protein trafficking and BBSome function to partially restore ciliary signaling across multiple organs. The final designed polypeptide (flagged as designed) conceptually delivers neurotrophic support to slow apoptotic photoreceptor loss and preserve retinal function. These approaches offer complementary mechanistic coverage with favorable physicochemical profiles (short lengths, stable residues) and synthetic feasibility, while the real-world polypeptide provides proven translational success.
