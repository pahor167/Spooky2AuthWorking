Xeroderma Pigmentosum (XP) is a rare autosomal recessive genetic disorder caused by mutations in genes responsible for the nucleotide excision repair (NER) pathway, which is essential for repairing DNA damage induced by ultraviolet (UV) radiation from sunlight. The primary genes affected include XPA, XPB, XPC, XPD, XPE, XPF, XPG, and XPV, leading to impaired recognition, incision, and resynthesis of damaged DNA strands. This results in accumulation of UV-induced lesions such as cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers and (6-4) photoproducts, promoting mutagenesis and carcinogenesis. Symptoms typically appear in early childhood and include extreme photosensitivity with severe sunburns, blistering, and erythema after minimal sun exposure, persistent hyperpigmentation or freckling on sun-exposed areas, dry and parchment-like skin (xerosis), telangiectasia, and a dramatically increased risk of developing skin cancers like basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and melanoma at a young age. Ocular manifestations may involve photophobia, conjunctivitis, keratitis, and increased risk of ocular surface cancers. In approximately 20-30% of cases, particularly those with mutations in XPA, XPB, XPD, or XPG, neurological complications arise, including progressive neurodegeneration, sensorineural hearing loss, cognitive impairment, ataxia, and peripheral neuropathy due to unrepaired oxidative DNA damage in non-dividing cells. Pathophysiology centers on defective NER leading to genomic instability, chronic inflammation from immune response to damaged cells, oxidative stress exacerbation, and premature cellular senescence, culminating in tissue degeneration and high cancer incidence, with XP patients having over 1000-fold increased risk of skin cancer and reduced life expectancy if unmanaged.
