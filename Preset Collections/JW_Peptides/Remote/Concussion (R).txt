Concussion, commonly referred to as mild traumatic brain injury (mTBI), is a type of head injury resulting from a sudden impact, acceleration, or deceleration force to the head, leading to temporary disruption of brain function without structural damage visible on standard imaging. Common causes include contact sports collisions, falls in the elderly or children, motor vehicle accidents, assaults, or blast exposures in military settings. Symptoms can manifest immediately or develop over hours to days and include physical issues like headache, nausea, vomiting, dizziness, balance problems, blurred vision, sensitivity to light or noise; cognitive impairments such as confusion, difficulty concentrating, memory lapses, slowed thinking; emotional changes like irritability, anxiety, depression, or mood swings; and sleep disturbances such as insomnia or excessive drowsiness. The pathophysiology of concussion involves a complex neurometabolic cascade: initial biomechanical forces cause axonal stretching and membrane disruption, leading to unregulated ion fluxes (e.g., potassium efflux, calcium influx), excitotoxicity from glutamate release, mitochondrial dysfunction, energy crisis with reduced ATP, oxidative stress from reactive oxygen species, neuroinflammation via microglial activation and cytokine release, impaired cerebral blood flow autoregulation, and potential blood-brain barrier compromise. These processes can result in secondary injury cascades, prolonging recovery and contributing to post-concussion syndrome in 10-20% of cases, where symptoms persist beyond 3 months. Vulnerability factors include age (younger brains more susceptible due to incomplete myelination), prior concussions (cumulative effects), and genetic factors like ApoE4 allele increasing risk of poor outcomes.
