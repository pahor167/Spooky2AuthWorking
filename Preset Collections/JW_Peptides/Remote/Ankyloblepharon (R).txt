Ankyloblepharon is a rare congenital ocular anomaly characterized by partial or complete fusion of the upper and lower eyelid margins, resulting in restricted or absent eyelid opening at birth. The most common variant is ankyloblepharon filiforme adnatum (AFA), featuring thin filamentous tissue bands connecting the eyelids, often allowing limited visibility but impairing full movement; other forms include partial or total fusion. It may occur isolated (sporadic or familial) or syndromically, notably in Hay-Wells/Ankyloblepharon-Ectodermal Defects-Clefting (AEC) syndrome due to heterozygous mutations in TP63, Van der Woude/popliteal pterygium syndromes linked to IRF6 mutations, or rarer associations with FOXE3, RIPK4 (CHAND syndrome), or other genes. Symptoms include potential amblyopia from visual deprivation if severe, exposure keratopathy, corneal ulceration, tearing abnormalities, and associated systemic features in syndromic cases (e.g., ectodermal dysplasia with sparse hair, dental anomalies, nail dystrophy, cleft lip/palate, skin erosions). Pathophysiology stems from disrupted fetal eyelid development: eyelids normally fuse around 8-9 weeks gestation for protection and separate by 20-28 weeks via coordinated apoptosis, epithelial keratinization, migration, and junction dissolution at the lid margins; mutations in key transcription factors (e.g., p63 regulating stratification, proliferation, and apoptosis; IRF6 influencing intercellular adhesion) or downstream effectors impair this separation, leading to persistent fusion or fibrotic bands, with possible secondary inflammation or scarring.
