Blood sugar balance, or glucose homeostasis, refers to the maintenance of blood glucose levels within a narrow physiological range (typically 70-140 mg/dL) to support cellular energy needs while preventing hyperglycemia (high blood sugar) or hypoglycemia (low blood sugar). Imbalances manifest in conditions like type 1 diabetes (autoimmune beta-cell destruction leading to insulin deficiency), type 2 diabetes (insulin resistance with relative insulin deficiency), prediabetes, or reactive hypoglycemia. Causes include genetic factors (e.g., mutations in insulin signaling genes), lifestyle (high-sugar diets, sedentary behavior), obesity (adipose-derived inflammation impairing insulin action), autoimmune processes, pancreatic dysfunction, hormonal dysregulation (e.g., excess cortisol or glucagon), and medications. Symptoms of hyperglycemia include polyuria, polydipsia, fatigue, blurred vision, slow wound healing, and long-term complications like neuropathy, retinopathy, nephropathy, and cardiovascular disease. Hypoglycemia presents with shakiness, sweating, confusion, seizures, or coma. Pathophysiologically, glucose homeostasis involves pancreatic beta-cells secreting insulin to promote glucose uptake in muscle/liver/adipose via GLUT4 translocation and suppress hepatic gluconeogenesis/glycogenolysis; alpha-cells release glucagon to counter low glucose by stimulating hepatic glucose output; incretins (GLP-1, GIP) from gut amplify postprandial insulin; amylin co-secreted with insulin slows gastric emptying and suppresses glucagon; and renal SGLT2 reabsorbs glucose, with excess excreted in urine. Dysregulation arises from beta-cell exhaustion, alpha-cell hyperactivity, incretin deficiency, or insulin resistance via inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-6) and oxidative stress impairing PI3K/Akt pathways.

The proposed polypeptides address key axes for blood sugar balance. Exenatide (real-world) targets the incretin pathway, mimicking GLP-1 to enhance glucose-dependent insulin secretion, suppress glucagon, and slow gastric emptying. Pramlintide (real-world) engages the amylin pathway, co-regulating with insulin to inhibit postprandial glucagon rise, delay nutrient absorption, and promote satiety. The designed polypeptides are conceptual C-terminally amidated 12-residue sequences: D1 (amphipathic) conceptually supports beta-cell function by membrane stabilization and potentiating insulin release signals; D2 (aromatic-cationic) conceptually modulates glucagon pathways through receptor interactions to fine-tune hepatic glucose output.
