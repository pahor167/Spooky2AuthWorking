Silicosis is an incurable, progressive interstitial lung disease primarily caused by long-term occupational exposure to respirable crystalline silica dust, such as quartz, through inhalation in industries including mining, quarrying, construction, sandblasting, and artificial stone fabrication. The disease manifests in acute, accelerated, or chronic forms, with chronic simple silicosis being the most common, developing after 10-15 years of low-level exposure, while acute silicosis can occur within months of high exposure. Symptoms typically include persistent dry cough, dyspnea (shortness of breath), chest pain, fatigue, fever, weight loss, and in severe cases, cyanosis, cor pulmonale, and respiratory failure. Complications encompass increased susceptibility to tuberculosis (silicotuberculosis), lung cancer, autoimmune disorders like rheumatoid arthritis or systemic sclerosis, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and kidney disease. Pathophysiologically, inhaled silica particles (0.5-5 {mu}m) deposit in the alveoli, where they are phagocytosed by alveolar macrophages, leading to lysosomal rupture, generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome, and release of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-1{beta}, IL-18, TNF-{alpha}, IL-6, and TGF-{beta}. This initiates a cascade of persistent inflammation, oxidative stress, endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress, apoptosis of alveolar epithelial cells, epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT), fibroblast-to-myofibroblast transition (FMT), excessive extracellular matrix (ECM) deposition including collagen and fibronectin, and ultimately irreversible pulmonary fibrosis with nodule formation and honeycombing. Additional mechanisms involve disrupted ATP metabolism in macrophages, signaling pathways like EGFR/JNK, IL33/ERK/AP-1, HMGB1/RAGE/{beta}-catenin, and PPAR{gamma}/LXR{alpha}, as well as the emergence of osteoclast-like cells contributing to tissue remodeling. Genetic factors, particle characteristics (e.g., freshness of fractured silica), and co-exposures (e.g., smoking) modulate disease severity. There is no curative treatment; management focuses on prevention, symptom relief, and lung transplantation in end-stage cases.
