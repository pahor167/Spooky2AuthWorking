Epidermolysis bullosa (EB) encompasses a group of rare inherited mechanobullous disorders characterized by extreme skin and mucosal fragility leading to blistering, erosions, and wounding from minimal friction or trauma. Mutations in at least 16 genes encoding structural proteins of the cutaneous basement membrane zone disrupt skin integrity across four major types: EB simplex (intraepidermal cleavage, often KRT5/KRT14 mutations affecting keratin intermediate filaments), junctional EB (junctional cleavage, commonly LAMA3/LAMB3/LAMC2 or integrin mutations impairing hemidesmosomes), dystrophic EB (sub-lamina densa cleavage, COL7A1 mutations causing defective type VII collagen anchoring fibrils), and Kindler EB (mixed cleavage levels, FERMT1 mutations disrupting kindlin-1/integrin activation). Symptoms range from mild localized blistering to severe widespread wounds, chronic ulceration, scarring, pseudosyndactyly (mitten deformities), milia, nail loss, oral/esophageal strictures, corneal abrasions, malnutrition, anemia, chronic pain, infection risk, and elevated squamous cell carcinoma incidence in severe forms; recessive dystrophic EB often leads to early mortality from aggressive malignancy or sepsis. Pathophysiology involves mechanical instability at specific skin layers triggering blister formation, secondary chronic inflammation with cytokine release, impaired wound healing, progressive fibrosis/scarring via TGF-{beta} upregulation, oxidative stress, and microbial colonization exacerbating tissue damage.
