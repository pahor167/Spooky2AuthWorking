Ebstein's Anomaly is a rare congenital heart defect characterized by the abnormal development of the tricuspid valve, where the septal and posterior leaflets are displaced apically towards the right ventricular apex, resulting in atrialization of a portion of the right ventricle. This malformation leads to tricuspid regurgitation, right ventricular dysfunction, and often associated anomalies such as atrial septal defects. Causes include genetic factors, with mutations in genes like MYH7 or NKX2.5 implicated in some cases, and potential environmental influences during fetal development. Symptoms vary by severity but commonly include cyanosis due to right-to-left shunting, heart palpitations from arrhythmias like Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome, fatigue, shortness of breath, and signs of right-sided heart failure such as edema and hepatomegaly. In severe cases, it can present neonatally with cardiomegaly and failure to thrive, while milder forms may be diagnosed in adulthood. Pathophysiology involves ineffective tricuspid valve closure causing regurgitation, reduced right ventricular output, increased right atrial pressure, and potential for arrhythmias due to accessory pathways or stretched atrial tissue. Over time, this leads to right ventricular dilation, fibrosis, and impaired cardiac output, potentially progressing to biventricular failure if left untreated.
