Pfeiffer syndrome is a rare genetic craniosynostosis disorder characterized by premature fusion of multiple cranial sutures, resulting in turribrachycephaly (tower-shaped skull with broad, short anteroposterior dimension), midface hypoplasia, shallow orbits with severe proptosis, hypertelorism, and airway compromise risks. Patients exhibit distinctive broad and radially deviated thumbs and great toes, partial syndactyly, and variable ankylosis of digits. Three clinical subtypes exist: Type 1 (classic, mild-moderate, good prognosis with surgical intervention), Type 2 (cloverleaf skull, severe proptosis, elbow ankylosis, higher neurodevelopmental risk), and Type 3 (similar to Type 2 but without cloverleaf skull, poor prognosis due to visceral anomalies and early lethality in severe cases). Additional features may include hydrocephalus, Chiari malformation, hearing loss, dental issues, and occasional intellectual disability in severe forms.

The condition arises from autosomal dominant gain-of-function mutations primarily in FGFR2 (most cases, especially Pro252Arg or Cys342 variants) and less commonly FGFR1, leading to constitutive receptor activation, enhanced ligand-independent dimerization, and prolonged downstream signaling via RAS/MAPK, PI3K/AKT, and PLC{gamma} pathways. This disrupts balanced osteoblast proliferation/differentiation at cranial sutures and endochondral/periosteal bone formation in limbs, promoting premature osteogenic commitment and suture obliteration while causing abnormal chondrogenesis and joint fusion.

Current management is supportive and surgical (cranial vault remodeling, midface advancement, digit separation), with no approved disease-modifying pharmacotherapy. Small-molecule FGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitors have shown preclinical promise in models but carry toxicity concerns for pediatric use.
