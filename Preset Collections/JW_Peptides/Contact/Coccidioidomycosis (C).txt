Coccidioidomycosis, commonly known as Valley Fever, is a systemic fungal infection caused by the dimorphic soil-dwelling fungi Coccidioides immitis or Coccidioides posadasii, endemic to arid regions such as the southwestern United States, Mexico, and parts of Central and South America. The disease is acquired through inhalation of airborne arthroconidia (spores) released from disturbed soil, which convert to pathogenic spherules in the lungs upon deposition in the alveoli, leading to rupture and release of endospores that propagate the infection. In immunocompetent hosts, the infection is often asymptomatic or self-limited, manifesting as flu-like symptoms including fever, cough, fatigue, headache, myalgia, and arthralgia; however, it can progress to acute pneumonia with pleuritic chest pain, hemoptysis, and infiltrates on imaging. Dissemination occurs in 1-5% of cases, particularly in immunocompromised individuals (e.g., HIV, transplant recipients, pregnant women, or those of African or Filipino descent), affecting extrapulmonary sites like skin (erythema nodosum or multiforme), bones/joints (osteomyelitis, arthritis), and CNS (meningitis with hydrocephalus, vasculitis). Pathophysiology involves evasion of host immunity: arthroconidia resist phagocytosis by alveolar macrophages, spherules inhibit oxidative burst and cytokine production (IL-12, IFN-{gamma}), while fungal proteases and urease contribute to tissue damage and alkalization favoring growth. Innate immunity (neutrophils, dendritic cells via Dectin-1/TLR2) and adaptive Th1/Th17 responses are critical for granuloma formation and clearance, but defects lead to chronic or disseminated disease. Diagnosis includes serology (IgM/IgG), culture, histopathology (spherules on silver stain), and PCR; complications include cavitary lung disease, fibrosis, and high mortality in CNS involvement (up to 30% even with treatment). Current management relies on azoles (fluconazole for mild/moderate, voriconazole/itraconazole for refractory), amphotericin B for severe/disseminated, with lifelong therapy for meningitis.
