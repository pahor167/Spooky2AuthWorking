Hereditary Neuropathy with Liability to Pressure Palsies (HNPP), also known as tomaculous neuropathy, is an autosomal dominant inherited demyelinating neuropathy primarily caused by a heterozygous deletion of the PMP22 gene on chromosome 17p12, leading to haploinsufficiency and reduced expression of peripheral myelin protein 22, a critical component for myelin compaction and stability in peripheral nerves. Genetic mutations or deletions disrupt myelin sheath integrity, resulting in focal hypermyelination (tomacula) that predisposes nerves to compression or trauma-induced demyelination. Environmental factors such as minor mechanical stress, repetitive motions, or positional pressure exacerbate episodes, while genetic modifiers may influence severity. Symptoms often manifest in adolescence or early adulthood as recurrent, transient episodes of focal sensory or motor deficits, including numbness, tingling, weakness, or pain in affected limbs, typically resolving over days to months but potentially leading to permanent deficits with repeated insults; common sites include peroneal, ulnar, or median nerves, mimicking entrapment neuropathies like carpal tunnel syndrome.
