CANDLE Syndrome, or Chronic Atypical Neutrophilic Dermatosis with Lipodystrophy and Elevated Temperature, is a rare autosomal recessive autoinflammatory disorder typically presenting in early infancy. It is primarily caused by loss-of-function mutations in PSMB8 or other genes encoding proteasome or immunoproteasome components (e.g., PSMA3, PSMB4, PSMB9, PSMB10, POMP), leading to impaired protein degradation and accumulation of ubiquitinated proteins. This triggers cellular stress responses, uncontrolled activation of type I interferon production, and a robust interferon signature, resulting in chronic systemic inflammation. Clinical features include recurrent high fevers, annular erythematous skin plaques with neutrophilic infiltrates, violaceous periorbital edema, progressive lipodystrophy (loss of subcutaneous fat, particularly facial), muscle atrophy, joint contractures, basal ganglia calcifications (in some cases), and failure to thrive. Symptoms often exacerbate with a chronic course, causing significant morbidity, including potential developmental delay and organ involvement.

The second designed polypeptide conceptually mitigates proteasomal stress by supporting protein clearance or reducing waste accumulation. The third designed polypeptide conceptually attenuates neutrophilic and cytokine-driven inflammation.
