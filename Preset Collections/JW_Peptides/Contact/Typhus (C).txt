Typhus encompasses a group of acute febrile illnesses caused by obligate intracellular Gram-negative bacteria of the Rickettsiaceae family, primarily Rickettsia prowazekii (epidemic typhus, transmitted by body lice), Rickettsia typhi (murine or endemic typhus, flea-borne from rodents), and Orientia tsutsugamushi (scrub typhus, chigger mite-borne). These diseases thrive in conditions of overcrowding, poor sanitation, and vector exposure, with epidemic typhus historically associated with wars and famines, murine typhus in urban rodent-infested areas, and scrub typhus in rural Asia-Pacific regions. Initial symptoms appear 7-14 days post-exposure and include sudden high fever (up to 40 C), severe headache, chills, myalgias, arthralgias, malaise, and a maculopapular rash starting on the trunk and spreading centrifugally (except in scrub typhus, where an eschar at the bite site is common). Advanced cases may involve delirium, confusion, stupor, seizures, pneumonia, myocarditis, hepatitis, renal failure, gangrene, and multi-organ dysfunction, with untreated mortality rates of 10-60% for epidemic typhus, 1-4% for murine, and up to 35% for scrub typhus. Pathophysiologically, rickettsiae adhere to and invade vascular endothelial cells via outer membrane proteins (e.g., OmpB), inducing actin polymerization for entry, escaping phagosomes, and replicating in the cytoplasm. This triggers endothelial activation, increased vascular permeability, fluid leakage, edema, hypotension, and microthrombi formation (vasculitis). Immune responses involve type 1-skewed inflammation with elevated cytokines (IL-8, TNF-{alpha}, IFN-{gamma}, IL-1{beta}), NLRP3 inflammasome activation, hemophagocytosis, and dendritic cell-mediated dissemination via CCR7. Additional axes include bacterial metabolic exploitation of host nutrients, neuroinflammation leading to encephalitis/meningitis, and pulmonary alveolar damage with interstitial pneumonia. Genetic factors (e.g., HLA types) and co-infections modulate severity. Standard treatment is doxycycline, but resistance emerges, necessitating novel therapeutics.
