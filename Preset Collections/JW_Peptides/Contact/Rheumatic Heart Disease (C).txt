Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD) is a serious complication arising from acute rheumatic fever (ARF), which is an inflammatory autoimmune disorder triggered by an untreated or inadequately treated infection with group A beta-hemolytic Streptococcus (GAS) bacteria, typically following pharyngitis. Predominantly affecting children and young adults in low- and middle-income countries, RHD develops when the immune system's response to GAS cross-reacts with host tissues, particularly cardiac valves, through molecular mimicry. Antibodies produced against streptococcal antigens, such as M protein, mistakenly target similar epitopes on cardiac proteins like myosin, laminin, and vimentin, leading to initial carditis. Repeated episodes of ARF exacerbate valve damage, causing chronic inflammation, fibrosis, scarring, calcification, and eventual valvular dysfunction, most commonly involving the mitral valve (stenosis or regurgitation), followed by the aortic valve. Risk factors include genetic predisposition (e.g., polymorphisms in immune response genes like HLA-DR), overcrowding, poverty, limited access to healthcare, and poor hygiene, which facilitate GAS transmission. Symptoms often manifest years after initial infection and include dyspnea on exertion, orthopnea, fatigue, palpitations, chest pain, edema, and signs of heart failure or atrial fibrillation due to left atrial enlargement. In severe cases, complications such as pulmonary hypertension, thromboembolism, infective endocarditis, and sudden cardiac death can occur. Pathophysiologically, the disease involves multiple axes: streptococcal infection persistence, autoimmune activation, chronic inflammation mediated by cytokines (TNF-{alpha}, IL-6, IL-1{beta}), oxidative stress from reactive oxygen species damaging valve endothelium, fibrotic remodeling via TGF-{beta} and matrix metalloproteinases leading to collagen deposition, valve calcification resembling atherosclerosis with calcium-phosphate accumulation, heart failure from volume/pressure overload, and impaired valve repair mechanisms. Without intervention, RHD leads to significant morbidity and mortality, with over 300,000 deaths annually worldwide.

The proposed polypeptides target these pathophysiological axes to prevent, mitigate, or manage RHD. Real-world polypeptides prioritize established or emerging options: J8 and J14, derived from conserved GAS M protein regions, elicit protective humoral immunity against streptococcal infection without inducing autoimmunity, addressing prevention and eradication of GAS to halt ARF progression; ANP and BNP, endogenous hormones, promote natriuresis, diuresis, and vasodilation to reduce cardiac preload and afterload, managing heart failure symptoms common in advanced RHD; Bivalirudin, a direct thrombin inhibitor, provides anticoagulation during valve surgery to prevent clots and support repair procedures; IL-2, at low doses, expands regulatory T cells to modulate autoimmune responses, suppressing cross-reactive antibodies and inflammation in RHD models; ProT{alpha} regulates immune responses and estrogen receptor activity, potentially reducing CD8+ T-cell mediated valve damage in RHD; IRW, an ovotransferrin-derived tripeptide, exerts antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects to combat oxidative stress and inflammation in cardiovascular tissues.
