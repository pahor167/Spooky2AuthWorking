Paget's Disease of Bone is a chronic skeletal disorder characterized by excessive and disorganized bone remodeling. The condition initiates with an overabundance of osteoclasts that are larger and more active than normal, leading to rapid bone resorption. In response, osteoblasts attempt to compensate by producing new bone at an accelerated rate; however, this new bone is structurally inferior, characterized by a 'mosaic' pattern of woven bone that is weaker and more vascular than healthy lamellar bone. This process results in bone enlargement, deformities, and an increased risk of fractures. Clinically, patients often present with bone pain, secondary osteoarthritis, and neurological complications if the skull or spine is involved.

The proposed polypeptides target the pathological acceleration of bone turnover through several mechanisms. Salmon Calcitonin and its analogs act as direct inhibitors of osteoclast activity by binding to G-protein coupled receptors on the osteoclast surface, rapidly reducing bone resorption. The OPG-derived fragments and Denosumab-inspired sequences serve as decoys or blockers for RANKL, the primary cytokine driving osteoclast differentiation. To manage the structural integrity of the bone, PTHrP and Sclerostin fragments are included to modulate osteoblast behavior and favor the formation of more organized bone tissue. Designed polypeptides like D1-RANK-Bind are engineered to competitively inhibit the RANK-RANKL interaction, while D2-Vasc-Stop addresses the hyper-vascularization typical of Pagetic lesions by mimicking anti-angiogenic signaling motifs.
