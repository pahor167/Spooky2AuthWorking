Hypertrichosis is a rare dermatological disorder characterized by excessive hair growth on various parts of the body, often exceeding what is considered normal for an individual's age, gender, ethnicity, and cultural norms. This condition can manifest as congenital hypertrichosis, present at birth due to genetic mutations affecting hair follicle development and cycling, or as acquired hypertrichosis, which develops later in life secondary to underlying systemic conditions, medications, or malignancies. Common causes include hormonal imbalances such as hyperandrogenism in polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), growth hormone excess in acromegaly, thyroid dysfunction, insulin resistance, and exposure to drugs like minoxidil or C-type natriuretic peptide analogs. Genetic forms may involve mutations in genes like ARID1B, ABCC9 (affecting potassium channels in Cantu syndrome), or FGF13, leading to dysregulated signaling pathways. Symptoms typically include dense, pigmented hair covering the face, trunk, limbs, and sometimes the entire body, resulting in significant psychological distress, social stigma, low self-esteem, and potential physical complications like skin irritation or infection from hair removal attempts. Pathophysiologically, hypertrichosis arises from perturbations in the hair follicle cycle, with prolonged anagen (growth) phase and shortened telogen (resting) phase, driven by overactivation of key signaling cascades such as Wnt/{beta}-catenin for stem cell activation, Sonic Hedgehog (Shh) for follicle morphogenesis, fibroblast growth factors (FGFs) for proliferation, and alterations in extracellular matrix components like proteoglycans in the dermal papilla. Hormonal influences exacerbate this by stimulating androgen receptors or insulin/IGF-1 pathways, while epigenetic modifications via histone acetylation or methylation can alter gene expression in hair-related genes. Additionally, ion channel dysfunctions and natriuretic peptide signaling can disrupt normal hair quiescence, leading to unchecked proliferation.

The proposed polypeptides provide a multifaceted mechanistic approach to addressing hypertrichosis by targeting its diverse pathophysiological axes, ensuring comprehensive coverage of hormonal, signaling, ion channel, and epigenetic mechanisms. Similarly, the real-world exenatide, a glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) analog, enhances insulin sensitivity and reduces hyperinsulinemia, which in turn lowers ovarian androgen production in conditions like PCOS, mitigating androgen-driven hirsutism; its feasibility is supported by clinical use in metabolic disorders, with good bioavailability via subcutaneous administration and favorable physicochemical properties for stability.
