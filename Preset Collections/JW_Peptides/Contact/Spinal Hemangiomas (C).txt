Spinal hemangiomas, commonly referred to as vertebral hemangiomas, are the most prevalent benign vascular lesions of the spine with an incidence of 10-12% in the general population based on autopsy and imaging studies, predominantly affecting the thoracic vertebrae though lumbar and cervical involvement occurs. These lesions arise from proliferations of thin-walled capillary-like or cavernous blood vessels lined by endothelium, infiltrating the bone marrow and surrounding remodeled trabeculae, often with abundant fatty stroma in typical cases; macroscopically they appear as honeycomb-like vascular spaces with sclerotic bone, while histologically they feature endothelial-lined channels amid edematous or fatty matrix and reactive bony changes. Most are asymptomatic incidental findings on imaging performed for unrelated reasons, presenting classic 'polka-dot' (axial) and 'corduroy' (sagittal) patterns due to thickened vertical trabeculae.
