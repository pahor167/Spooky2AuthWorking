Cystinuria is an autosomal recessive genetic disorder primarily caused by mutations in the SLC3A1 (encoding rBAT) or SLC7A9 (encoding b0,+AT) genes, which form the heterodimeric amino acid transporter responsible for reabsorbing cystine and the dibasic amino acids lysine, arginine, and ornithine in the proximal renal tubules and small intestine. These mutations lead to impaired transport, resulting in excessive urinary excretion of cystine (cystinuria) and the dibasic amino acids. Cystine has low solubility in urine at physiological pH (below 7), leading to supersaturation, precipitation, and formation of cystine crystals and stones in the urinary tract. Symptoms typically begin in childhood or early adulthood and include recurrent flank pain, hematuria, urinary tract infections, nausea, vomiting, and in severe cases, obstructive uropathy leading to hydronephrosis, chronic kidney disease, hypertension, or end-stage renal disease. Pathophysiology involves multiple axes: defective amino acid transport causing hypercystinuria; cystine supersaturation and crystallization due to low solubility; oxidative stress from cystine accumulation exacerbating cellular damage in renal tubules; inflammatory responses triggered by stone formation and obstruction, including cytokine release and neutrophil infiltration; fibrotic remodeling in the kidney from repeated injury; disrupted metabolic homeostasis, such as altered sulfur metabolism; and potential secondary complications like infections and impaired renal function. Environmental factors, such as low fluid intake or high sodium/protein diet, worsen stone formation. Diagnosis includes urine analysis for cystine levels (>250 mg/L), stone composition, genetic testing, and imaging for stones.
