Myotonic Dystrophy, also known as Steinert's disease, is a multisystemic genetic disorder and the most common form of muscular dystrophy in adults, affecting approximately 1 in 8,000 individuals worldwide. It is caused by an expansion of CTG trinucleotide repeats in the 3' untranslated region of the DMPK gene on chromosome 19 for type 1 (DM1), or CCTG repeats in the CNBP gene for type 2 (DM2). In DM1, the expanded repeats lead to toxic RNA gain-of-function, forming hairpin structures that sequester RNA-binding proteins like MBNL, resulting in splicing defects, and activating pathways such as PKC and GSK3{beta} hyperphosphorylation of CELF1. Symptoms include muscle weakness, myotonia (delayed muscle relaxation), cataracts, cardiac conduction abnormalities, insulin resistance, cognitive impairment, and gastrointestinal issues. Pathophysiology involves RNA toxicity causing mis-splicing of genes like CLCN1 (leading to myotonia via reduced chloride conductance), altered signaling (e.g., reduced AMPK, increased autophagy), and ion channel dysfunction. There is no cure, but management focuses on symptoms.

The proposed polypeptides address Myotonic Dystrophy through multiple therapeutic axes: targeting toxic RNA to prevent hairpin formation and protein sequestration, correcting splicing defects by modulating MBNL availability, modulating ion channels to restore membrane excitability, inhibiting aberrant signaling pathways like GSK3{beta}, and enhancing gene therapy delivery. Designed polypeptides D1-D5 are conceptualized to bind CUG repeats, stabilize splicing factors, interact with chloride channels, inhibit kinases, and act as delivery enhancers, respectively, potentially reducing RNA foci, restoring normal splicing (e.g., CLCN1, BIN1), normalizing ion conductance, balancing pathways (e.g., AKT/AMPK), and improving therapeutic uptake, thus alleviating myotonia, weakness, and systemic effects.
