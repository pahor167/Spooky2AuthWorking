Erdheim-Chester Disease (ECD) is a rare multisystem non-Langerhans cell histiocytosis characterized by the abnormal proliferation and infiltration of lipid-laden macrophages or histiocytes into various tissues and organs, leading to inflammation, fibrosis, and organ dysfunction. The exact etiology remains incompletely understood, but it is primarily driven by somatic mutations in genes involved in the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) pathway, most commonly the BRAF V600E mutation, which occurs in approximately 50-60% of cases. Other mutations in genes such as NRAS, KRAS, MAP2K1, and PIK3CA have also been identified, contributing to dysregulated cell signaling, uncontrolled histiocyte proliferation, and chronic inflammation. Environmental or infectious triggers have been hypothesized but not confirmed. Symptoms are highly variable depending on the organs affected, often including bone pain from symmetric osteosclerotic lesions in long bones, exophthalmos due to retro-orbital infiltration, diabetes insipidus from pituitary involvement, dyspnea and pulmonary fibrosis from lung infiltration, cardiovascular issues such as pericardial effusion or coated aorta, neurological deficits including ataxia or cognitive impairment from central nervous system (CNS) lesions, and skin manifestations like xanthelasmas. This leads to multisystem organ failure if untreated, with CNS and cardiovascular involvement being major prognostic factors for poor outcomes. Diagnosis relies on biopsy showing characteristic histiocytes, imaging (e.g., PET-CT revealing hypermetabolic lesions), and molecular testing for mutations.
