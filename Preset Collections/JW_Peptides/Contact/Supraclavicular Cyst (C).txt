Supraclavicular cysts are uncommon benign cystic lesions presenting as soft, fluctuant, often painless or intermittently tender masses in the supraclavicular fossa, predominantly on the left side. They encompass several entities sharing lymphatic or developmental origins, including thoracic duct cysts (dilations of the terminal thoracic duct due to congenital wall weakness or acquired degeneration/inflammation), cystic lymphangiomas (malformations with aberrant lymphatic vessel proliferation), and less commonly branchial cleft cysts extending to the supraclavicular region (persistent embryonic remnants). Symptoms include visible or palpable swelling that may enlarge rapidly (especially postprandial in thoracic duct cases due to chyle accumulation), recur spontaneously, or rarely complicate with infection, rupture, or compression. Pathophysiology involves impaired lymphatic drainage, fluid accumulation from wall incompetence, or failed embryonic involution, leading to isolated or multiloculated cystic spaces filled with lymph or chyle. Differential includes other neck cysts, but imaging (US, CT, MRI) shows characteristic fluid-filled, non-enhancing lesions near venous angles or posterior cervical space. Standard management is observation for asymptomatic cases, aspiration/sclerotherapy (e.g., OK-432), or surgical excision for persistent/symptomatic/recurrent cysts, with excellent prognosis post-complete removal.
