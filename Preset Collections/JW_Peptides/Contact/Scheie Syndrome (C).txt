Scheie syndrome (MPS IS) is the attenuated (mildest) phenotypic form of mucopolysaccharidosis type I (MPS I), an autosomal recessive lysosomal storage disorder caused by biallelic pathogenic variants in the IDUA gene encoding alpha-L-iduronidase, resulting in profoundly reduced or absent enzyme activity. This leads to progressive intralysosomal accumulation of incompletely degraded glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), specifically dermatan sulfate and heparan sulfate, in various tissues. Clinical onset is typically after age 5, with preserved intelligence and normal life expectancy distinguishing it from intermediate (Hurler-Scheie) and severe (Hurler) forms. Characteristic features include joint stiffness and contractures (often affecting hands/shoulders), carpal tunnel syndrome, corneal clouding causing visual impairment, mild coarse facial features, aortic and mitral valve thickening/regurgitation, short stature, spinal deformities, and occasional airway obstruction or hearing loss. Unlike severe MPS I, there is no significant central nervous system involvement, cognitive impairment, or hepatosplenomegaly.

Pathophysiology involves GAG deposition disrupting connective tissue architecture (joints, valves), extracellular matrix integrity, and cellular signaling, triggering secondary inflammation, fibrosis, and tissue remodeling. Standard management includes enzyme replacement therapy with recombinant full-length alpha-L-iduronidase (laronidase), surgical interventions (e.g., carpal tunnel release, valve replacement, corneal transplant), physical/occupational therapy, and monitoring.
