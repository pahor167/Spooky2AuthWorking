Schindler Disease, also known as alpha-N-acetylgalactosaminidase deficiency or Kanzaki disease in its adult form, is an ultra-rare autosomal recessive lysosomal storage disorder caused by mutations in the NAGA gene, leading to deficient activity of the lysosomal enzyme alpha-N-acetylgalactosaminidase (alpha-NAGAL). This enzyme is essential for the degradation of O-linked glycoproteins and glycolipids containing terminal alpha-N-acetylgalactosamine residues. The deficiency results in the accumulation of undegraded glycopeptides and glycolipids in lysosomes, particularly in neurons, Schwann cells, and skin cells, triggering cellular dysfunction, oxidative stress, inflammation, and progressive tissue damage. The disease manifests in three phenotypic variants: Type I (infantile-onset neuroaxonal dystrophy) with severe neurodegeneration, psychomotor retardation, hypotonia, seizures, and early death; Type II (adult-onset Kanzaki disease) characterized by angiokeratoma corporis diffusum, mild intellectual impairment, and peripheral neuropathy; and Type III (intermediate form) with milder neurocognitive deficits and seizures. Pathophysiology involves impaired lysosomal catabolism, leading to lysosomal swelling, impaired autophagy, secondary mitochondrial dysfunction, neuroinflammation via microglial activation, axonal dystrophy, and demyelination in the central and peripheral nervous systems. Systemic effects include skin lesions due to vascular endothelial accumulation and potential renal or cardiac involvement in advanced cases. Genetic mutations, often missense leading to protein misfolding and ER retention, exacerbate the enzyme deficiency, with no curative treatments currently available-management is supportive, focusing on symptom alleviation.
