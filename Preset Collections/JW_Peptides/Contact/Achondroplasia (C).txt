Achondroplasia is the most common form of skeletal dysplasia and disproportionate short stature, primarily caused by an autosomal dominant gain-of-function mutation in the fibroblast growth factor receptor 3 (FGFR3) gene, most commonly the G380R mutation. This mutation leads to constitutive activation of FGFR3, a tyrosine kinase receptor expressed in chondrocytes of the growth plate, resulting in excessive intracellular signaling through pathways such as MAPK/ERK and STAT1. This overactivation inhibits chondrocyte proliferation, differentiation, and hypertrophy, impairing endochondral ossification and leading to shortened bones, particularly in the limbs. Symptoms typically appear at birth and include short stature with rhizomelic shortening (shorter proximal limbs), macrocephaly with frontal bossing, midface hypoplasia, trident hands, exaggerated lumbar lordosis, and genu varum (bow legs). Complications can arise throughout life, including foramen magnum stenosis potentially causing hydrocephalus or respiratory issues in infancy, sleep apnea, spinal stenosis leading to neurological symptoms in adults, and orthopedic problems like joint pain and limited mobility. The condition affects approximately 1 in 25,000 births and is diagnosed through clinical examination, radiographic findings (e.g., narrowed interpedicular distance in the spine, square iliac wings), and genetic testing. Pathophysiologically, the mutant FGFR3 disrupts the normal balance of growth plate dynamics, including reduced matrix production and altered interactions with other signaling molecules like Indian hedgehog (IHH) and parathyroid hormone-related peptide (PTHrP), further exacerbating growth impairment. While lifespan is normal, quality of life can be impacted by physical limitations and social challenges, emphasizing the need for multidisciplinary management including monitoring for complications, physical therapy, and surgical interventions like limb lengthening or spinal decompression when necessary.

The proposed polypeptides address achondroplasia by targeting key therapeutic axes to counteract the overactive FGFR3 signaling and restore normal bone growth.
