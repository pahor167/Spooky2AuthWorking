Paraneoplastic syndromes are rare disorders triggered by an altered immune system response to a neoplasm, often preceding the diagnosis of the underlying cancer. They are not caused by direct tumor invasion, metastasis, or treatment side effects, but rather by remote effects of the tumor, such as secretion of hormones, peptides, cytokines, or immune cross-reactivity between tumor and normal tissues. Common types include neurological (e.g., paraneoplastic cerebellar degeneration, Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome), endocrine (e.g., Cushing's syndrome, syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion, hypercalcemia), dermatological (e.g., acanthosis nigricans, dermatomyositis), hematological (e.g., Trousseau's syndrome, paraneoplastic pemphigus), and rheumatological (e.g., hypertrophic osteoarthropathy). Symptoms vary by type: neurological may include ataxia, neuropathy, cognitive impairment; endocrine - weight gain, hyponatremia, bone pain; dermatological - rash, muscle weakness; hematological - thrombosis, anemia; rheumatological - joint pain, clubbing. Pathophysiology involves two main mechanisms: (1) tumor production of bioactive substances (e.g., PTHrP in hypercalcemia, ACTH in Cushing's); (2) immune-mediated (e.g., onconeural antibodies in neurological PNS). The proposed polypeptides address these through targeted mechanisms: real-world polypeptides like octreotide, DOTATOC, DOTATATE, and calcitonin inhibit ectopic hormone effects and manage symptoms in endocrine PNS by binding somatostatin receptors to suppress hormone secretion or lowering calcium levels.
