The condition typically results from inactivating mutations in the CASR gene (FHH Type 1), which encodes the calcium-sensing receptor (CaSR) responsible for monitoring extracellular calcium levels. In FHH, the receptor's 'set point' is shifted, meaning the body requires higher-than-normal calcium levels to signal the parathyroid glands to suppress PTH and the kidneys to excrete calcium. While usually benign and asymptomatic, FHH must be carefully distinguished from primary hyperparathyroidism to avoid unnecessary surgical interventions like parathyroidectomy, which are ineffective for FHH.

3. Endocytic Trafficking Regulation (AP2{sigma} Support): FHH Type 3 is caused by mutations in AP2S1, affecting receptor internalization. Targeting the adaptor protein complex 2 (AP2) facilitates proper CaSR trafficking and recycling to the cell surface, ensuring a sufficient receptor population is available for sensing.
4. PTH-Receptor Antagonism: In cases where mild PTH elevation contributes to symptomatic bone resorption, modulating the PTH1 receptor can mitigate systemic calcium release from the skeletal reservoir.
5. Renal TRPV6 Channel Modulation: The CaSR regulates the TRPV6 channel, essential for renal calcium reabsorption. Peptides that inhibit excessive TRPV6-mediated calcium uptake in the distal tubule can help correct the hypocalciuric profile.
