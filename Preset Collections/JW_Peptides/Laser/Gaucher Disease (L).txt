Gaucher disease is the most common lysosomal storage disorder, caused by biallelic mutations in the GBA1 gene leading to deficient activity of the lysosomal enzyme {beta}-glucocerebrosidase (GCase). This deficiency results in progressive accumulation of glucosylceramide (GlcCer) and its deacylated toxic derivative glucosylsphingosine (lyso-GlcCer or GluSph) primarily in macrophages, forming characteristic lipid-laden 'Gaucher cells.' Pathophysiology involves visceral organ enlargement (hepatosplenomegaly), bone marrow infiltration causing cytopenias (anemia, thrombocytopenia), skeletal complications (osteopenia, infarcts, fractures), pulmonary involvement, and in neuronopathic forms (types 2 and 3) severe central nervous system degeneration with seizures, cognitive impairment, and early lethality. Secondary mechanisms include chronic inflammation driven by cytokine release (IL-1{beta}, IL-6, TNF-{alpha}), complement activation, oxidative stress, and in GBA1 heterozygotes an elevated risk for Parkinson's disease via impaired {alpha}-synuclein clearance and bidirectional pathogenic loops.

Approved therapies include enzyme replacement therapy (recombinant GCase infusions) and substrate reduction therapy (glucosylceramide synthase inhibitors), which effectively manage visceral and hematologic manifestations in type 1 but show limited CNS penetration for neuronopathic forms.
