Refsum Disease is an ultra-rare autosomal-recessive peroxisomal disorder affecting fewer than 1 in 10 million individuals worldwide. The disease results from defective {alpha}-oxidation of phytanic acid, a branched-chain fatty acid primarily found in ruminant fats and dairy products. Mutations in PHYH (encoding phytanoyl-CoA hydroxylase) or PEX7 (encoding the peroxisomal targeting signal receptor) prevent normal phytanic acid metabolism, leading to its systemic accumulation in blood, liver, brain, retina, and peripheral nerves. This accumulation drives a slowly progressive multi-organ syndrome characterized by retinitis pigmentosa causing night blindness and tunnel vision, chronic sensorimotor polyneuropathy, cerebellar ataxia, sensorineural hearing loss, ichthyosis, and potentially fatal cardiac arrhythmias. The retinal degeneration begins in childhood with rod photoreceptor dysfunction, while neurological symptoms typically emerge in the second decade.

The proposed polypeptide therapeutic strategy addresses multiple pathophysiological mechanisms beyond current dietary restriction and plasmapheresis approaches. The antioxidant peptide provides cysteine-rich motifs that scavenge reactive oxygen species generated by accumulated phytanic acid. The anti-inflammatory peptide reduces neuroinflammation that exacerbates peripheral neuropathy and retinal degeneration. The neuroprotective peptide supports neuronal survival through growth factor signaling pathways. This multi-modal approach targets both the primary metabolic defect and secondary pathological processes, offering comprehensive neuroprotection that could slow progression regardless of the specific PHYH or PEX7 mutation.
