Clinically, it manifests with greatly increased risk of Wilms tumor (approximately 50-60% lifetime risk due to loss of WT1 tumor suppressor function in renal progenitors), aniridia (absent or malformed iris from PAX6 loss, often with secondary glaucoma, cataracts, corneal opacification, and vision impairment), genitourinary anomalies (e.g., hypospadias, cryptorchidism, renal malformations, gonadoblastoma risk, stemming from WT1's role in urogenital differentiation), and a range of intellectual disabilities or developmental delays (mild to severe, influenced by the deletion extent and possible BDNF involvement). The condition is sporadic in most cases, with incidence around 1 in 500,000-1,000,000 births, and diagnosis is confirmed by chromosomal microarray or FISH showing 11p13 deletion.
