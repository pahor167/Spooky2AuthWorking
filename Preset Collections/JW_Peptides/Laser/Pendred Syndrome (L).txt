Pendred syndrome is a rare autosomal recessive genetic disorder characterized by bilateral sensorineural hearing loss, often profound and present from birth or early childhood, associated with inner ear malformations such as enlarged vestibular aqueduct (EVA) or Mondini dysplasia, and thyroid goiter that typically develops in late childhood or adolescence, with variable hypothyroidism. Additional features may include vestibular dysfunction leading to balance issues, vertigo, or delayed motor milestones. The condition accounts for up to 10-15% of syndromic hereditary deafness, with prevalence estimated at 1-9 per 100,000, and is caused by biallelic pathogenic variants in the SLC26A4 gene encoding pendrin, a chloride-iodide/bicarbonate anion exchanger critical for ion homeostasis in the inner ear endolymph and thyroid iodide efflux/organification. Diagnosis involves audiometry, temporal bone imaging (CT/MRI for EVA), perchlorate discharge test, thyroid ultrasound/function tests, and SLC26A4 sequencing.
