A dog heart murmur is an abnormal sound detected during cardiac auscultation, resulting from turbulent blood flow within the heart or great vessels. It is a common finding in veterinary medicine, affecting dogs of all ages, breeds, and sizes, though prevalence increases with age. The primary causes include acquired conditions such as myxomatous mitral valve disease (MMVD), which is the most frequent in small to medium-sized breeds like Cavalier King Charles Spaniels, where the mitral valve thickens and prolapses, leading to regurgitation and left atrial enlargement. Congenital defects, such as patent ductus arteriosus (PDA), ventricular septal defect (VSD), pulmonic stenosis, or subaortic stenosis, are more common in puppies and certain breeds like Boxers or Golden Retrievers. Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) predominates in large breeds like Doberman Pinschers, causing ventricular dilation and systolic dysfunction. Other etiologies encompass infective endocarditis from bacterial infections (e.g., Bartonella, Staphylococcus), heartworm disease (Dirofilaria immitis) causing pulmonary hypertension, extracardiac factors like anemia (reducing blood viscosity) or hyperthyroidism (increasing cardiac output), and physiologic innocent murmurs in puppies due to high flow states or anemia. Symptoms vary by severity and underlying cause; mild cases may be asymptomatic, while advanced disease presents with exercise intolerance, coughing from pulmonary edema, lethargy, syncope, cyanosis, abdominal distension from ascites, tachypnea, and weight loss. Pathophysiology involves disrupted laminar blood flow, leading to pressure overload, volume overload, or both, triggering compensatory mechanisms like renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) activation, sympathetic stimulation, and natriuretic peptide release. Chronic overload results in eccentric or concentric hypertrophy, fibrosis, remodeling, arrhythmias, and eventual congestive heart failure (CHF), characterized by fluid retention, reduced cardiac output, and multi-organ dysfunction. Diagnosis includes physical exam (murmur grading I-VI based on intensity, timing, location), echocardiography for structural assessment, radiographs for cardiomegaly or pulmonary changes, ECG for arrhythmias, and blood tests (e.g., NT-proBNP as biomarker). Management focuses on the underlying cause, with medical therapy for CHF using pimobendan, ACE inhibitors, diuretics, and beta-blockers, surgical interventions for congenital defects, and supportive care like diet and exercise restriction.
