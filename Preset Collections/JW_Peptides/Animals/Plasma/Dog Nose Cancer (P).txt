Dog nose cancer, also known as canine nasal tumors, encompasses a range of malignant neoplasms primarily affecting the nasal cavity, paranasal sinuses, and occasionally extending to the nasal planum or adjacent structures. Predominant histological types include adenocarcinomas (most common, often arising from glandular epithelium), sarcomas (such as chondrosarcomas or osteosarcomas), squamous cell carcinomas, and less frequently melanomas or lymphomas. This condition is more prevalent in older dogs (median age 9-10 years) and dolichocephalic breeds like collies, shepherds, and retrievers, though any breed can be affected. Etiological factors include chronic exposure to environmental carcinogens (e.g., tobacco smoke, urban pollutants), genetic predispositions, viral influences (e.g., potential oncogenic viruses), and chronic rhinitis or sinusitis leading to metaplastic changes. Symptoms typically present as unilateral or bilateral epistaxis (nosebleeds), chronic nasal discharge (mucopurulent or hemorrhagic), sneezing, facial deformity or asymmetry due to bone erosion, exophthalmos (bulging eyes) from orbital invasion, dyspnea, halitosis, anorexia, and neurological signs like seizures if intracranial extension occurs. Metastasis occurs in 10-40% of cases, primarily to regional lymph nodes or lungs, with poor prognosis (median survival 3-6 months without treatment, extending to 8-19 months with radiation). Diagnosis involves rhinoscopy, CT/MRI for staging, biopsy for histopathology, and exclusion of differentials like aspergillosis or foreign bodies.
