Equine helminth infections, commonly requiring deworming, are caused by ingestion of infective larval stages from contaminated pasture, water, or feed, leading to establishment of gastrointestinal parasites. Primary pathogens include cyathostomins (small strongyles, now the most prevalent and problematic due to widespread resistance), Parascaris species (ascarids, especially in foals), Anoplocephala perfoliata (tapeworms), and occasionally Oxyuris equi (pinworms) or large strongyles (largely controlled historically). Symptoms range from subclinical weight loss, poor coat condition, and reduced performance to severe colic, diarrhea, anemia, and life-threatening larval cyathostominosis (massive synchronous emergence of encysted larvae causing intense intestinal inflammation and protein-losing enteropathy). Pathophysiology involves mucosal damage from migrating or feeding larvae/adults, inflammation, impaired nutrient absorption, and in heavy burdens, thromboembolism or intestinal wall thickening.

Current treatments rely on small-molecule anthelmintics (macrocyclic lactones, benzimidazoles, pyrantels, praziquantel combinations) but face extensive resistance, particularly in cyathostomins, limiting efficacy and necessitating targeted selective therapy based on fecal egg counts. No polypeptide-based anthelmintics are currently approved or routinely used in equine practice, as evidenced by comprehensive literature and database searches showing dominance of chemical classes with no peptide therapeutics or investigational polypeptide candidates specific to equine helminths.
