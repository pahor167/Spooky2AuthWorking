Dog acid reflux, or gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) in canines, is a common gastrointestinal disorder characterized by the backward flow of stomach acid and contents into the esophagus, leading to irritation and inflammation of the esophageal lining. This condition can be triggered by various factors including a weakened lower esophageal sphincter, hiatal hernia (particularly in brachycephalic breeds), anesthesia, obesity, dietary indiscretions, or underlying issues like hypothyroidism or upper airway obstruction that create thoracoabdominal pressure gradients. Symptoms typically include regurgitation, vomiting, excessive salivation (ptyalism), difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), weight loss, and signs of pain such as whining or restlessness. Pathophysiologically, the exposure of the esophagus to acidic gastric contents causes mucosal damage, erosion, and potential complications like esophagitis, strictures, or laryngospasm. The neuronal nitric oxide synthase-mast cell axis plays a role in mucosal defense, while impaired E-cadherin junctional adhesion exacerbates tissue injury. Therapeutic approaches focus on reducing acid exposure, enhancing motility, protecting the mucosa, and mitigating inflammation. The proposed polypeptides address these axes: real-world ones like BPC-157 promote mucosal healing and protection, while designed ones target acid suppression and anti-inflammatory pathways to restore esophageal integrity and prevent reflux episodes. Designed polypeptides are clearly flagged as conceptual and aim to inhibit proton pumps or modulate mast cell activity for comprehensive relief.
