Dog Kennel Cough, or Canine Infectious Respiratory Disease Complex (CIRDC), is a highly contagious upper respiratory infection in dogs caused by multiple pathogens including Bordetella bronchiseptica, Canine Parainfluenza Virus, and others like Mycoplasma or Adenovirus. It spreads through airborne droplets in crowded environments like kennels, presenting symptoms such as a persistent dry hacking cough, retching with white foam, nasal discharge, sneezing, and mild fever, though severe cases can progress to pneumonia. Pathophysiology involves pathogen adhesion to ciliated epithelium, toxin release impairing mucociliary clearance, local inflammation, and secondary bacterial invasions leading to tracheobronchitis.
The proposed polypeptides combat Dog Kennel Cough by focusing on antibacterial clearance, antiviral interference, and mucosal protection. For antiviral modulation, a designed polypeptide inhibits viral fusion proteins akin to those in parainfluenza, preventing epithelial cell infection and spread; conceptual basis includes short inhibitory peptides from human models, ensuring low immunogenicity. For anti-inflammatory and mucosal repair, the real-world LL-37 fragment (shortened to fit criteria) promotes innate immunity while dampening excessive cytokine release, aiding in ciliary regeneration and symptom relief; as a cathelicidin derivative, it addresses the inflammatory core of the disease complex.
