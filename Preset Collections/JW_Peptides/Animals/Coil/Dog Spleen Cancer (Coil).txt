Dog spleen cancer, predominantly hemangiosarcoma (HSA), is an aggressive vascular malignancy originating from endothelial cells lining blood vessels, frequently presenting in the spleen as the primary site in canines. This condition is more common in middle-aged to older dogs (median age 9-11 years) and breeds such as German Shepherds, Golden Retrievers, Labrador Retrievers, and Boxers, with a slight male predisposition. Etiological factors include genetic mutations (e.g., in PTEN, TP53, PI3K/AKT pathways), potential environmental exposures like ultraviolet radiation or chemicals leading to oncogenic transformations, and idiopathic causes. Symptoms often manifest acutely due to splenic rupture, including lethargy, pale gums, abdominal distension from hemoabdomen (internal bleeding), tachycardia, hypotension, anorexia, and collapse; chronic cases may show weight loss, intermittent weakness, or palpable abdominal masses. The disease progresses rapidly with high metastatic potential (80-90% at diagnosis) to liver, lungs, heart, and omentum, leading to poor prognosis (median survival 19-86 days post-splenectomy alone, extending to 141-179 days with adjuvant doxorubicin-based chemotherapy).
