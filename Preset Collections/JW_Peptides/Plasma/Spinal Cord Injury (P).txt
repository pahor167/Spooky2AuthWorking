Spinal Cord Injury (SCI) is a severe neurological disorder caused by traumatic or non-traumatic damage to the spinal cord, resulting in disruption of neural signaling between the brain and the body. Primary causes include high-impact trauma from motor vehicle accidents, falls, sports-related incidents, gunshot wounds, or violence, as well as non-traumatic factors such as degenerative diseases, tumors, infections, vascular issues, or inflammatory conditions like transverse myelitis. Symptoms depend on the injury level and completeness: cervical injuries often lead to tetraplegia, respiratory compromise, and autonomic dysfunction; thoracic injuries cause paraplegia with preserved upper body function; lumbar or sacral injuries affect lower limbs, bowel, bladder, and sexual function. Common manifestations include loss of motor control, sensory deficits, chronic pain, spasticity, bowel/bladder incontinence, sexual dysfunction, and secondary complications like pressure ulcers, infections, or cardiovascular issues. Pathophysiology begins with primary mechanical injury (contusion, compression, laceration) that directly damages neurons, axons, glia, and vasculature, followed by a secondary cascade involving ischemia from vascular disruption, excitotoxicity via glutamate overload, oxidative stress from reactive oxygen species, inflammation driven by microglia/astrocyte activation and cytokine release (e.g., TNF-{alpha}, IL-6), apoptosis/necrosis/pyroptosis of neurons and oligodendrocytes, demyelination impairing signal conduction, glial scar formation by reactive astrocytes inhibiting axonal regrowth, and chronic maladaptive plasticity contributing to neuropathic pain. This multifaceted process leads to permanent functional deficits, with limited spontaneous regeneration in the adult CNS due to inhibitory molecules like Nogo-A, myelin-associated glycoprotein, and chondroitin sulfate proteoglycans. Without effective interventions, SCI profoundly impacts quality of life, independence, and socioeconomic status, often requiring lifelong supportive care.

The proposed polypeptides target key pathophysiological mechanisms in SCI, including neuroinflammation, cell death, axonal regeneration, neuronal survival, remyelination, angiogenesis, vascular integrity, oxidative stress, neuropathic pain, glial scarring, autophagy, Sonic Hedgehog signaling, and chemokine pathways, with scientific rationale based on preclinical studies showing improved motor recovery, tissue preservation, and reduced secondary damage. Feasibility involves local intrathecal or systemic delivery, with modes of action via receptor modulation (e.g., RhoA inhibition for growth, BDNF upregulation for survival), anti-inflammatory cytokine suppression, antioxidant effects, and physicochemical properties like high solubility, stability in CSF, and low immunogenicity for therapeutic use. Designed polypeptides (D1-remy, D2-gut, D3-shh, D4-chem) are conceptual to cover gaps: D1-remy promotes oligodendrocyte maturation and myelin sheath formation to restore conduction; D2-gut modulates microbiota-derived LPS to reduce systemic inflammation exacerbating SCI; D3-shh activates hedgehog pathway for neurogenesis and axonal guidance; D4-chem inhibits CCL2/CCR2 signaling to limit microglia recruitment and neuroinflammation.
