Holoprosencephaly (HPE) is a severe congenital malformation of the forebrain characterized by incomplete cleavage of the prosencephalon into distinct cerebral hemispheres, occurring between the 18th and 28th days of gestation. It presents on a spectrum from alobar (complete failure of division, single ventricle, absent interhemispheric fissure) to semilobar (partial division) and lobar (near-complete division with fused frontal lobes), with middle interhemispheric variant as a milder form. Causes include genetic mutations in key developmental genes (e.g., SHH, ZIC2, SIX3, TGIF) leading to disrupted signaling pathways like Sonic Hedgehog (SHH), Nodal/TGF{beta}, and FGF, as well as environmental factors such as maternal diabetes, alcohol exposure, or teratogens like cyclopamine. Symptoms vary by severity but often include cyclopia or closely spaced eyes in severe cases, midline facial defects (cleft lip/palate), developmental delays, seizures, endocrine dysfunction (e.g., diabetes insipidus), hydrocephalus, and motor impairments. Pathophysiology stems from impaired ventral midline patterning, resulting in fused basal ganglia, absent corpus callosum, hypothalamic-pituitary axis anomalies, and neuronal migration defects, leading to high mortality in severe forms (up to 50% in first year) and significant neurodevelopmental challenges in survivors.

The proposed designed polypeptides target core therapeutic axes in HPE to potentially mitigate developmental disruptions. For SHH signaling enhancement, the designed polypeptide conceptually acts as a cyclic enhancer to boost SHH pathway activity, promoting midline cleavage and reducing forebrain fusion. In the Nodal/TGF{beta} pathway, a designed modulator aims to stabilize signaling for proper prosencephalon division and neural axis determination. For FGF signaling, a mimic design supports fibroblast growth factor cascades to aid in forebrain patterning and reduce associated craniofacial anomalies. These flagged designed polypeptides consider physicochemical stability for potential prenatal delivery, with feasibility grounded in pathway modulation, though requiring further preclinical validation to assess safety and efficacy in addressing genomic instability and teratogenic effects.
