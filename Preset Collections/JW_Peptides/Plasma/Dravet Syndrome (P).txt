Dravet syndrome, also known as severe myoclonic epilepsy of infancy, is a rare and devastating developmental and epileptic encephalopathy with onset typically in the first year of life in an otherwise healthy infant. It is characterized by initial prolonged, often unilateral, febrile seizures triggered by hyperthermia (e.g., fever, warm baths), progressing to multiple afebrile seizure types including myoclonic jerks, atypical absences, focal impaired awareness seizures, tonic-clonic, and tonic seizures, frequently with episodes of status epilepticus or obtundation. Seizures are highly drug-resistant, leading to frequent emergencies. Beyond epilepsy, patients exhibit developmental regression or plateau after seizure onset, resulting in moderate to severe intellectual disability, autism spectrum features, attention deficits, hyperactivity, motor impairments (crouch gait, ataxia, pyramidal signs), sleep disturbances, dysautonomia, and growth issues. There is a markedly elevated risk of sudden unexpected death in epilepsy (SUDEP), estimated at 10-20% by adulthood, often linked to seizure-related cardiorespiratory failure. Incidence is approximately 1 in 15,000-40,000 births, with over 80% of cases due to de novo pathogenic variants in SCN1A causing haploinsufficiency of the Nav1.1 voltage-gated sodium channel, predominantly impairing fast-spiking GABAergic inhibitory interneurons while sparing excitatory neurons, thus disrupting excitatory-inhibitory balance and promoting network hyperexcitability. Remaining cases involve mutations in related genes (e.g., GABRA1, PCDH19) or are genetically undefined.

Pathophysiology involves reduced sodium currents in interneurons, leading to diminished inhibitory synaptic transmission, hippocampal and cortical hyperexcitability, thalamo-cortical dysrhythmia, and secondary neuroinflammation/astrogliosis contributing to comorbidities. Standard management prioritizes seizure reduction with valproate, clobazam, stiripentol, cannabidiol, and fenfluramine, alongside avoidance of exacerbating sodium channel blockers, emergency protocols, ketogenic diet in some, and supportive therapies; emerging genetic approaches (ASOs, gene regulation) are in trials but not polypeptide-based.
