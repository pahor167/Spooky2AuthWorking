A meniscal tear is a common knee injury involving damage to the meniscus, the C-shaped fibrocartilage that cushions and stabilizes the femur and tibia, distributing load and facilitating smooth movement. Causes include acute trauma (twisting or hyperflexion with the foot planted, often in sports) or degenerative changes (age-related wear, obesity, or repetitive stress leading to fraying). Symptoms include localized pain (medial or lateral), swelling, effusion, mechanical catching/locking, popping sensations, instability, and reduced range of motion. Pathophysiology centers on the meniscus's limited healing potential: the outer 'red zone' has vascular supply enabling some repair via extrinsic pathways, while the inner 'avascular white zone' relies on poor intrinsic healing with low cellularity and nutrient diffusion, resulting in persistent tears. Tears trigger local inflammation with pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, TNF-{alpha}), catabolic enzymes (MMPs, ADAMTS), matrix degradation, impaired collagen/proteoglycan synthesis, synovial inflammation, and progressive joint degeneration, often accelerating posttraumatic osteoarthritis with cartilage loss, subchondral changes, and chronic pain.

Standard treatments range from conservative (rest, ice, NSAIDs, physical therapy) to surgical (arthroscopic partial meniscectomy or repair), but outcomes vary due to poor spontaneous healing in avascular regions, risk of OA progression post-meniscectomy, and resistance to full restoration.
