Hypoxemic hypoxia, also known as arterial hypoxemia leading to tissue hypoxia, is a condition characterized by reduced oxygen content in arterial blood (low PaO2), resulting in inadequate oxygen delivery to tissues despite normal or compensated cardiac output. It manifests with symptoms such as dyspnea, tachycardia, cyanosis, confusion, headache, and in severe cases, organ dysfunction or failure. Common causes include hypoventilation (e.g., opioid overdose, neuromuscular disease), ventilation-perfusion mismatch (e.g., pneumonia, pulmonary embolism), right-to-left shunt (e.g., ARDS, congenital heart defects), diffusion impairment (e.g., interstitial lung disease, pulmonary edema), and low inspired oxygen (e.g., high altitude). Pathophysiology involves decreased alveolar oxygen transfer, activation of peripheral chemoreceptors driving hyperventilation, erythropoietin release for polycythemia in chronic cases, and cellular adaptation via hypoxia-inducible factors (HIFs) that upregulate genes for angiogenesis (VEGF), glycolysis, and erythropoiesis; unchecked or severe hypoxia leads to inflammation, oxidative stress, endothelial dysfunction, and cell death.

Peptide 1 (designed) is glycine-proline rich to conceptually reinforce alveolar-capillary barrier integrity and enhance oxygen diffusion. Peptide 2 (designed) is arginine-lysine rich cationic for potential cell penetration and suppression of hypoxic inflammation. Peptide 3 (designed) is serine-glutamine rich polar to conceptually support epithelial repair and metabolic resilience. Peptide 4 (designed) is phenylalanine-tyrosine rich aromatic for conceptual ROS scavenging in oxidative stress. Peptide 5 is the real-world synthetic vasoactive intestinal peptide Aviptadil, with phase II/III evidence in COVID-19 ARDS for improving oxygenation via pulmonary anti-inflammatory, bronchodilatory, and cytokine-modulating effects. These sequences prioritize synthetic accessibility and potential inhaled/IV delivery for lung targeting and rapid action.
