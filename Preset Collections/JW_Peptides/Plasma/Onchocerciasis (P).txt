Onchocerciasis, also known as river blindness, is a parasitic disease caused by the filarial nematode Onchocerca volvulus, transmitted through repeated bites by blackflies of the genus Simulium. The causes include infection with O. volvulus microfilariae that migrate through the skin and eyes, leading to intense itching, skin depigmentation, nodules, and ocular lesions. Symptoms include severe pruritus, dermatitis, subcutaneous nodules, lymphadenitis, and in advanced cases, visual impairment or blindness due to corneal opacity, uveitis, and optic atrophy. Pathophysiology involves the release of Wolbachia bacterial endosymbionts upon microfilarial death, triggering inflammatory responses via TLR4, eosinophil activation, cytokine storms (IL-5 axis), and immune evasion by the parasite, resulting in chronic inflammation, fibrosis, and neurological complications like epilepsy in some regions.

The proposed polypeptides include real-world epitope peptides from O. volvulus vaccine candidates that address immune modulation to enhance protective responses.
