Hereditary Angioedema (HAE) is a rare autosomal dominant genetic disorder characterized by recurrent episodes of severe swelling (angioedema) affecting the limbs, face, gastrointestinal tract, and upper airways, often triggered by trauma, stress, or spontaneously, with potentially life-threatening laryngeal involvement leading to asphyxiation. It is primarily caused by mutations in the SERPING1 gene encoding C1-inhibitor (C1-INH), resulting in type I (quantitative deficiency) or type II (functional deficiency) HAE, or less commonly by mutations in F12 (factor XII), PLG (plasminogen), ANGPT1, KNG1, or unknown genes leading to HAE with normal C1-INH. Pathophysiology involves dysregulation of the kallikrein-kinin system, where reduced C1-INH activity fails to inhibit plasma kallikrein, leading to excessive bradykinin production, which binds B2 receptors causing vascular permeability and edema; complement activation and contact system overactivity exacerbate this, with bradykinin as the key mediator. Symptoms include non-pitting, non-pruritic swelling lasting 1-5 days, abdominal pain mimicking surgical emergencies, and fatigue/prostration, with variable onset from childhood to adulthood and incomplete penetrance influenced by estrogens.

The proposed polypeptides address HAE through therapeutic axes: bradykinin B2 receptor antagonism to block edema formation, kallikrein inhibition to curb bradykinin generation, C1-INH mimicry to regulate complement and kinin pathways, factor XII inhibition to prevent contact system activation, and plasminogen modulation to stabilize fibrinolysis in relevant subtypes. The designed polypeptides D1-D4 are conceptualized to inhibit kallikrein, mimic C1-INH regulatory domains, block FXII, and modulate plasminogen, respectively, potentially normalizing bradykinin levels, reducing attack frequency/severity, and improving quality of life with fewer side effects than current therapies.
