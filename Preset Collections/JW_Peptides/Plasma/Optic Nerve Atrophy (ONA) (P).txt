Optic Nerve Atrophy (ONA) is the end-stage result of various pathological processes that lead to the degeneration of retinal ganglion cell (RGC) axons and the subsequent loss of vision. The condition is characterized by the pallor of the optic disc, reflecting the loss of capillary supply and the replacement of nerve fibers with glial tissue. Causes range from glaucoma and ischemia to genetic mutations (e.g., Leber’s Hereditary Optic Neuropathy) and traumatic injury. The pathophysiology involves a cascade of events including oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and the activation of apoptotic pathways in RGCs. Because the central nervous system has limited regenerative capacity, the therapeutic focus is on neuroprotection to save surviving neurons and axonal regeneration to restore lost connections. The proposed polypeptides address these challenges through four primary therapeutic axes. Colivelin and the designed polypeptide D1 target neuroprotective pathways by suppressing pro-apoptotic signaling and activating survival factors like STAT3. Axonal regeneration is addressed by NEP1-40 and the designed polypeptide D2, which aim to neutralize inhibitory signals in the optic nerve environment and stimulate intrinsic growth programs. Mitochondrial support is provided by SS-31 and the designed polypeptide D3, which stabilize the mitochondrial membrane and reduce reactive oxygen species (ROS) production. Finally, Peptide 6 and the designed polypeptide D4 offer neurotrophic support, mimicking growth factors to sustain neuronal health and promote functional recovery.
