Botulism is a rare, serious, and potentially life-threatening neuroparalytic illness caused primarily by the potent neurotoxins produced by the anaerobic, spore-forming bacterium Clostridium botulinum, and less commonly by related species such as C. butyricum or C. baratii. The disease manifests through seven main forms: foodborne (ingestion of preformed toxin in contaminated food), wound (toxin production from bacterial infection in wounds), infant (intestinal colonization and toxin production in infants under 1 year due to immature gut flora), adult intestinal toxemia (similar to infant but in adults with altered gut microbiota), iatrogenic (from therapeutic or cosmetic botulinum toxin injections), inhalational (rare, from aerosolized toxin), and hidden (undetermined source). The botulinum neurotoxin (BoNT), a 150 kDa protein consisting of a heavy chain (HC) for binding and translocation and a light chain (LC) zinc-endopeptidase, irreversibly cleaves SNARE proteins (e.g., SNAP-25 for BoNT/A, E; VAMP/synaptobrevin for BoNT/B, D, F, G; syntaxin for BoNT/C) essential for synaptic vesicle fusion, thereby inhibiting acetylcholine release at neuromuscular junctions, autonomic cholinergic synapses, and peripheral ganglia. This leads to symmetric descending flaccid paralysis starting with cranial nerve palsies (ptosis, diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia), progressing to limb weakness, respiratory muscle paralysis requiring mechanical ventilation, and autonomic symptoms (dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation). Without treatment, mortality can reach 60% from respiratory failure; with supportive care and antitoxin, it drops to <5%. Pathophysiology involves toxin absorption into circulation, binding to neuronal receptors (gangliosides and synaptic proteins like SV2 or synaptotagmin), receptor-mediated endocytosis, HC-mediated LC translocation into cytosol, and LC proteolytic activity, with recovery depending on nerve terminal sprouting over months.

The proposed polypeptides address botulism through multiple mechanistic axes: toxin neutralization (binding circulating BoNT to prevent neuronal entry), receptor binding inhibition (blocking HC-receptor interactions), translocation inhibition (preventing LC cytosolic entry), intracellular protease inhibition (directly blocking LC endopeptidase activity on SNAREs), and bacterial eradication (antimicrobial action against C. botulinum to halt toxin production in wound/infant forms). Real-world polypeptides, primarily small peptide inhibitors derived from substrate mimics or screening, target intracellular inhibition by competitively binding the LC active site, reducing SNARE cleavage and preserving neurotransmitter release. Due to limited real-world options (only 8 verifiable with sequences <=150 residues; larger antitoxins like BAT exceed length limits), designed polypeptides supplement, clearly flagged, incorporating non-standard residues (e.g., ornithine, diaminopropionic acid, hydroxyproline, selenocysteine, 2-aminobutyric acid) for enhanced potency, membrane penetration, stability against proteolysis, and selectivity; cyclic structures via disulfide bonds improve conformational rigidity and bioavailability. Modes of action are supported by in vitro proteolytic assays, cell-based SNAP-25 cleavage models, and in vivo mouse paralysis delay studies, with potential for post-exposure efficacy where antitoxins fail intracellularly.
