Chronic Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS), formerly known as reflex sympathetic dystrophy, is a debilitating chronic pain condition that typically develops after an injury, surgery, or even minor trauma to a limb, though it can occasionally occur without an identifiable trigger. It is classified into CRPS Type I (without confirmed nerve injury) and Type II (with nerve damage, also called causalgia). Causes include peripheral nerve trauma leading to aberrant signaling, exaggerated inflammatory responses involving cytokines and neuropeptides, autoimmune mechanisms with autoantibodies targeting adrenergic and muscarinic receptors, mitochondrial dysfunction contributing to oxidative stress and energy deficits in affected tissues, and psychological factors exacerbating pain perception. Genetic predispositions, such as variations in HLA genes or cytokine pathways, may increase susceptibility, while environmental factors like immobilization or stress can precipitate onset. Symptoms manifest regionally, often in a limb, and include severe burning pain disproportionate to the inciting event, allodynia (pain from non-painful stimuli like light touch), hyperalgesia (amplified pain response), edema, changes in skin temperature/color (warm/red or cold/blue phases), sweating abnormalities, motor impairments like weakness or dystonia, and trophic changes such as hair/nail growth alterations or skin atrophy. In advanced stages, it can spread to contralateral or distant body parts, leading to disability, depression, and reduced quality of life. Pathophysiologically, CRPS involves a cascade of interconnected mechanisms: initial peripheral sensitization from nerve injury releases neuropeptides like substance P and CGRP, triggering neurogenic inflammation with cytokine upregulation (IL-1{beta}, IL-6, TNF-{alpha}) and mast cell activation; this evolves into central sensitization with enhanced spinal cord and brain excitability via NMDA receptor activation and glial cell involvement; sympathetic dysregulation causes vaso- and sudomotor abnormalities through sympatho-afferent coupling; autoimmune processes contribute via IgG autoantibodies sensitizing nociceptors; mitochondrial dysfunction impairs cellular energy and promotes ROS-mediated damage; and altered emotional processing in brain regions like the anterior cingulate cortex amplifies affective pain components, creating a vicious cycle of maladaptive neuroplasticity.

The proposed polypeptides target these axes to alleviate CRPS. For inflammation, the designed polypeptide modulates cytokine release and mast cell activity to reduce neurogenic swelling and pain. For central/peripheral sensitization, the designed polypeptide inhibits NMDA signaling and glial activation to dampen hyperalgesia and allodynia. For sympathetic dysregulation, the designed polypeptide regulates adrenergic receptor function to normalize autonomic responses and prevent vaso-sudomotor changes. For neuromodulation, the designed polypeptide enhances endogenous inhibitory pathways to restore neural balance and reduce spontaneous pain. For autoimmune aspects, the designed polypeptide interferes with autoantibody binding to minimize immune-mediated nociceptor sensitization. MOTS-c addresses mitochondrial dysfunction by optimizing biogenesis, reducing oxidative stress, and improving energy metabolism in affected neurons and tissues. The designed polypeptide for mitochondrial further supports organelle dynamics and ROS scavenging. For emotional processing, the designed polypeptide targets affective brain circuits to uncouple sensory-emotional pain components, reducing distress amplification. Feasibility is supported by preclinical models demonstrating reduced inflammation, normalized autonomic function, and improved pain scores, with physicochemical properties like short length ensuring stability, bioavailability, and targeted delivery without systemic side effects.
