Lesch-Nyhan Syndrome (LNS) is a rare X-linked recessive genetic disorder caused by mutations in the HPRT1 gene, resulting in severe deficiency or complete absence of hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase (HGPRT) enzyme activity. This disrupts the purine salvage pathway, leading to excessive de novo purine synthesis, uric acid overproduction, and consequent hyperuricemia, gouty arthritis, nephrolithiasis, and potential renal failure. Neurologically and behaviorally, LNS is characterized by extrapyramidal dystonia, choreoathetosis, spasticity, dysarthria, and most distinctly, compulsive self-injurious behaviors (e.g., lip/finger biting, head banging) that emerge in childhood despite patients often sensing pain normally. Cognitive impairment varies but is common, with basal ganglia dysfunction central to the motor and behavioral phenotype. Pathophysiology includes dopaminergic neuron deficits (reduced striatal dopamine), impaired energy metabolism in midbrain dopaminergic cells, oxidative stress, and dysregulated purinergic signaling, though the precise link between purine metabolism and neurobehavioral symptoms remains incompletely understood.
