X-Linked Agammaglobulinemia (XLA) is a primary immunodeficiency disorder caused by mutations in the BTK gene on the X chromosome, leading to impaired B cell development and maturation, resulting in absent or very low levels of circulating B cells and immunoglobulins. This genetic defect disrupts B cell signaling, preventing the production of antibodies essential for humoral immunity. Symptoms typically emerge in infancy or early childhood, including recurrent bacterial infections such as otitis media, sinusitis, pneumonia, and gastrointestinal infections due to encapsulated bacteria like Streptococcus pneumoniae and Haemophilus influenzae. Chronic infections can lead to complications like bronchiectasis, enteroviral meningoencephalitis, and increased risk of malignancies or autoimmune phenomena. Pathophysiology centers on the failure of pre-B cells to progress to mature B cells in the bone marrow, with secondary effects on innate immunity including neutrophil and monocyte dysfunction, contributing to persistent inflammation and tissue damage.
