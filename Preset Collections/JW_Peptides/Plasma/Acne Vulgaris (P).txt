Acne vulgaris is a prevalent chronic inflammatory dermatosis primarily affecting adolescents and young adults, though it can persist or emerge in adulthood. It manifests as non-inflammatory lesions (open and closed comedones), inflammatory lesions (papules, pustules, nodules), and in severe forms, cysts and scarring, predominantly on the face, upper trunk, and back. Symptoms include pain, tenderness, and significant psychosocial impact due to visible disfigurement. The pathophysiology centers on four interconnected axes: androgen-driven hypersecretion of sebum by sebaceous glands, abnormal hyperproliferation and retention hyperkeratosis of follicular keratinocytes leading to comedo formation, follicular colonization and proliferation of Cutibacterium acnes with biofilm formation, and subsequent innate and adaptive immune responses triggering release of pro-inflammatory cytokines, chemokines, and matrix metalloproteinases that amplify tissue damage.

The proposed polypeptides comprehensively address these core pathogenic axes through targeted mechanisms, offering potential for multimodal therapy with improved tolerability over conventional agents. Peptide 2 (designed) incorporates polar and proline-rich motifs to conceptually promote orderly keratinocyte differentiation and desquamation, countering follicular plugging. Peptides 3 and 4 target the bacterial axis: Peptide 3 is the real-world investigational synthetic antimicrobial peptide Omiganan, a cathelicidin analog with documented potent bactericidal activity against Cutibacterium acnes via membrane permeabilization; Peptide 4 (designed) is an amphipathic cationic sequence conceptually optimized for similar membrane-disrupting action to complement coverage. Peptide 5 (designed) features residues associated with immunomodulatory motifs to conceptually suppress pro-inflammatory cytokine cascades and neutrophil recruitment. All designed polypeptides are clearly flagged as conceptual hypotheses; the real polypeptide Omiganan has established preclinical and phase II clinical evidence for topical use in acne. These short sequences favor skin penetration, proteolytic resistance relative to longer proteins, and synthetic feasibility, supporting potential topical formulation.
