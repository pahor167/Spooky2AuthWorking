Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially fatal systemic hypersensitivity reaction that develops rapidly after exposure to a trigger allergen in sensitized individuals, involving multiple organ systems and characterized by symptoms such as urticaria, angioedema, dyspnea, wheezing, hypotension, tachycardia, nausea, vomiting, and potential cardiovascular collapse or airway obstruction. Primary triggers include foods (e.g., peanuts, shellfish), insect venoms, medications (e.g., beta-lactams, NSAIDs), and biologics, with pathophysiology dominated by massive degranulation of mast cells and basophils releasing preformed mediators (histamine, tryptase) and newly synthesized ones (leukotrienes, prostaglandins, PAF, cytokines), leading to vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, bronchoconstriction, and immune cell recruitment. While the classical pathway is IgE/Fc{epsilon}RI-mediated, non-IgE mechanisms (MRGPRX2 activation, complement, IgG) contribute in some cases, amplifying neurogenic and inflammatory components.

The proposed polypeptides target three key therapeutic axes: (1) direct inhibition of mast cell degranulation to limit primary mediator release; (2) immunomodulatory suppression of cytokine and inflammatory signaling; (3) cytoprotective stabilization of vascular integrity to counteract shock. No established small real-world polypeptide therapies exist for anaphylaxis within length constraints, as standard acute treatment relies on epinephrine and large monoclonal antibodies (e.g., omalizumab) are used preventively in related conditions; all candidates are designed polypeptides derived from literature motifs with modifications for drug-likeness.

The first designed polypeptide is a head-to-tail cyclic analog derived from the catestatin motif of chromogranin A, a natural inhibitor of nicotinic-induced mast cell degranulation, with cyclization for enhanced proteolytic stability and bioavailability. The second is a VIP-derived analog based on vasoactive intestinal peptide with C-terminal amidation, leveraging its established suppression of mast cell activation and cytokine production for immunomodulatory effects. The third is a PACAP-derived analog from pituitary adenylate cyclase-activating polypeptide with C-terminal amidation, drawing on its preclinical protective roles against inflammatory damage and vascular dysfunction.
