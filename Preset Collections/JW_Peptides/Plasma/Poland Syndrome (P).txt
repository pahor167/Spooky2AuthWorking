Poland Syndrome (also known as Poland anomaly or pectoral hand syndrome) is a rare congenital disorder occurring in approximately 1 in 20,000 to 100,000 births, characterized primarily by unilateral hypoplasia or aplasia of the pectoralis major muscle, often with associated ipsilateral upper extremity anomalies. Common features include absence of the sternocostal head of the pectoralis major, hypoplasia of the pectoralis minor, subcutaneous tissue deficiency, breast/nipple-areola complex underdevelopment (particularly in females), and hand deformities such as syndactyly, brachydactyly, ectrodactyly, or hypoplasia of the middle phalanges. Less frequently, it involves rib cage anomalies, dextrocardia, or lung herniation. The condition is typically sporadic, more common on the right side and in males, with rare familial clustering. Pathophysiology remains incompletely elucidated but is hypothesized to involve early embryonic vascular disruption (subclavian artery supply disruption syndrome hypothesis around weeks 6-8 of gestation), leading to ischemic hypoplasia of affected mesenchymal tissues; alternative theories include genetic contributions (e.g., possible roles of TBX5 or other loci) or environmental factors, though no consistent monogenic cause has been identified. Clinical impacts include chest asymmetry with potential functional limitations in shoulder mobility, hand grip/dexterity deficits, cosmetic concerns, and psychosocial effects; no preventive measures or curative medical therapies exist, with management limited to surgical reconstruction (e.g., muscle flaps, implants, fat grafting for chest; separation/release procedures for hand).
