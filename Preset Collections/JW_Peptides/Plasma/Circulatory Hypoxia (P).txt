Circulatory hypoxia, also termed stagnant or ischemic hypoxia, arises when systemic or regional blood flow is insufficient to deliver adequate oxygen to tissues despite normal arterial oxygenation and hemoglobin levels. It commonly occurs in shock states (cardiogenic, hypovolemic, distributive/septic, obstructive), congestive heart failure, vascular occlusion, microcirculatory dysfunction, or severe anemia with compensatory limits. Symptoms include fatigue, confusion, oliguria, cool peripheries, lactic acidosis, and progressive multi-organ dysfunction; severe cases lead to cell death via ATP depletion, ionic imbalance, and reperfusion injury upon restoration. Pathophysiology involves reduced perfusion pressure, endothelial activation with glycocalyx shedding, microvascular thrombosis, leukocyte adhesion, nitric oxide dysregulation, oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and inflammatory cytokine release amplifying tissue damage.

The proposed polypeptides address key therapeutic axes in circulatory hypoxia through multimodal conceptual mechanisms suited to short synthetic sequences. Peptide 2 (designed) is glycine-proline rich to conceptually protect endothelial barrier and microcirculation. Peptide 3 (designed) is serine-glutamine rich polar for conceptual suppression of hypoperfusion-triggered inflammation. Peptide 4 (designed) is phenylalanine-tyrosine rich aromatic to conceptually mitigate oxidative damage in low-flow states. Peptide 5 is the real-world human Angiotensin II (Giapreza), FDA-approved synthetic octapeptide with established clinical use in vasodilatory shock for potent vasoconstriction, blood pressure restoration, and improved tissue perfusion. These diverse short designs prioritize feasibility, potential rapid onset, and compatibility with standard resuscitation.
