Diverticulosis refers to the formation of numerous small, bulging pouches (diverticula) in the lining of the digestive tract, most commonly in the lower portion of the large intestine (sigmoid colon). It develops when weak spots in the colonic wall give way under elevated intraluminal pressure, allowing the mucosa and submucosa to herniate outward. The condition is highly prevalent in Western populations over age 50, often linked to low-fiber diets that result in smaller stool volume, slower transit, constipation, and segmental high-pressure contractions. Additional contributing factors include age-related changes in connective tissue (altered collagen crosslinking and elastin), smooth muscle thickening, disordered motility with exaggerated segmentation, chronic low-grade inflammation, and gut microbiome dysbiosis favoring pro-inflammatory species. While frequently asymptomatic, it may present with nonspecific symptoms like abdominal discomfort, bloating, or irregular bowel habits; complications such as diverticulitis (inflammation or infection of diverticula), abscess, perforation, bleeding, or fistula can arise, though the proposed polypeptides focus on prevention and management of underlying mechanisms rather than acute complications.
