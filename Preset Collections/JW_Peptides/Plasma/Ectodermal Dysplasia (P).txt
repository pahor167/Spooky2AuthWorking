Ectodermal dysplasia (ED) is a heterogeneous group of inherited disorders characterized by abnormalities in the development of ectodermal-derived structures, including the skin, hair, nails, teeth, sweat glands, salivary glands, and lacrimal glands. The condition is primarily genetic, with over 200 subtypes identified, the most common being X-linked hypohidrotic ectodermal dysplasia (XLHED) caused by mutations in the EDA gene, as well as other forms linked to genes like EDAR, EDARADD, and WNT10A. Causes include mutations that disrupt key signaling pathways such as EDA/EDAR/NF-kB, WNT, FGF, and BMP, leading to impaired ectoderm-mesoderm interactions during embryogenesis. Symptoms vary by subtype but often include sparse or absent hair (hypotrichosis), missing or malformed teeth (hypodontia or anodontia), reduced or absent sweating (hypohidrosis or anhidrosis) leading to heat intolerance, dry and thin skin prone to eczema or erosions, brittle or absent nails, and occasionally respiratory issues due to abnormal mucous glands or skeletal abnormalities in certain types like cranioectodermal dysplasia. Pathophysiology involves defective morphogenesis of ectodermal appendages, resulting from altered gene expression in signaling molecules that regulate cell proliferation, differentiation, and apoptosis in ectodermal tissues. Complications can include recurrent infections, dental problems affecting nutrition, overheating, and psychosocial issues due to cosmetic alterations.
