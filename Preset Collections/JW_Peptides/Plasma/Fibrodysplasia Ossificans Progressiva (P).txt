Fibrodysplasia Ossificans Progressiva (FOP) is an ultra-rare genetic disorder characterized by progressive heterotopic ossification (HO), where soft tissues such as muscles, tendons, and ligaments abnormally convert into bone, leading to severe disability and restricted mobility. The primary cause is a gain-of-function mutation in the ACVR1 gene (typically R206H), which encodes the ALK2 receptor, rendering it hypersensitive to ligands like Activin A and BMPs, triggering aberrant Smad1/5/8 signaling and ectopic bone formation. Triggers include trauma, inflammation, or spontaneous flare-ups with swelling and pain; symptoms manifest early with congenital great toe malformations, followed by episodic HO that locks joints, impairs respiration, and reduces lifespan. Pathophysiology involves dysregulated BMP signaling, enhanced by inflammation (mast cells, macrophages), oxidative stress, mTOR amplification, NF-{kappa}B activation, and TGF-{beta} pathways, culminating in endochondral ossification. Untreated, FOP leads to immobility by adulthood, with complications like restrictive lung disease.

Feasibility: Short sequences ensure stability, low immunogenicity, potential intramuscular delivery for flare-up prevention, but require preclinical testing for specificity and biodistribution; physicochemical properties include neutral charge for tissue penetration.
