Oculopharyngeal Muscular Dystrophy (OPMD) is a rare, late-onset autosomal dominant muscle disorder affecting approximately 1 in 100,000 individuals worldwide. The disease is caused by trinucleotide repeat expansions in the PABPN1 gene, resulting in an N-terminal expanded polyalanine tract in polyA-binding protein nuclear 1 (PABPN1). This leads to the formation of insoluble nuclear aggregates in skeletal muscles, particularly affecting the eyelid levator and pharyngeal muscles, causing progressive ptosis, dysphagia, and proximal limb weakness. The pathophysiology involves protein aggregation, impaired proteasome function, muscle fibrosis, and atrophy, with characteristic intranuclear inclusions in muscle biopsies.

The proposed polypeptide therapeutic strategy targets multiple pathophysiological mechanisms beyond current gene therapy approaches. The myostatin inhibitor peptide blocks the negative regulator of muscle mass, preventing muscle atrophy and improving muscle strength. The anti-aggregation peptide mimics normal PABPN1 structure to prevent polyalanine tract aggregation and inclusion formation. The proteasome activating peptide enhances the ubiquitin-proteasome system to improve clearance of misfolded proteins. The muscle regeneration peptide promotes muscle fiber repair and growth through growth factor signaling. The anti-fibrosis peptide reduces collagen deposition and muscle fibrosis that impairs muscle function. This multi-modal approach addresses both the primary protein aggregation defect and secondary muscle degeneration processes.
