Birt-Hogg-Dubé (BHD) syndrome is a rare autosomal dominant genetic disorder caused by germline pathogenic variants in the FLCN gene on chromosome 17p11.2, encoding folliculin, a tumor suppressor protein that interacts with FNIP1/FNIP2 to regulate multiple cellular processes. Folliculin deficiency leads to dysregulation of key pathways, including hyperactivation of mTORC1 signaling, impaired AMPK-mediated energy sensing, altered TFEB/TFE3-driven lysosomal biogenesis and autophagy, disrupted PGC-1{alpha} mitochondrial function, and aberrant HIF transcriptional activity. Clinically, it presents with cutaneous manifestations (multiple fibrofolliculomas and trichodiscomas, typically appearing after age 20 as dome-shaped papules on the face, neck, and upper trunk), pulmonary involvement (multiple thin-walled lung cysts, predominantly basilar, with high risk of recurrent spontaneous pneumothoraces), and renal tumors (often bilateral, multifocal chromophobe, oncocytic, or hybrid oncocytic/chromophobe renal cell carcinomas, with lifetime risk up to 30%). Less common features include parotid oncocytosis or colorectal polyps/neoplasia in some families. Incidence is estimated at 1-9 per million, often underdiagnosed, with variable penetrance and expressivity.

Pathophysiology centers on loss of folliculin's role in cellular homeostasis, promoting uncontrolled cell growth in susceptible tissues (kidney), cystic degradation via matrix metalloproteinase upregulation (lung), and hamartomatous proliferation (skin). Management is surveillance-based: annual renal MRI from age 21, pneumothorax risk counseling (avoid smoking/diving), dermatologic monitoring, and interventional treatments (pleurodesis for recurrent pneumothorax, ablative/surgical for renal tumors, laser for cosmetic skin lesions). Trials with mTOR inhibitors (e.g., everolimus) showed limited efficacy; no preventive or curative therapies exist.

Comprehensive searches across PubMed, clinicaltrials.gov, patents, and databases confirmed no documented real-world polypeptide or peptide therapies for BHD syndrome or its pathways. Thus, the proposed set comprises entirely designed conceptual polypeptides. These short peptides are rationally designed for potential cellular uptake, stability, and pathway-specific effects:

- The first designed peptide (D1) features cationic and hydrophobic residues to potentially modulate mTORC1 hyperactivity, supporting tumor suppression in renal cells.

- The second designed peptide (D2) is amphipathic to conceptually stabilize lung epithelial extracellular matrix and reduce cyst formation propensity.

- The third designed peptide (D3) incorporates motifs for potential hair follicle structural support, addressing fibrofolliculoma development.

- The fourth designed peptide (D4) includes residues aimed at enhancing energy-sensing signals, compensating for AMPK/TFEB dysregulation.

Feasibility considerations: lengths of 10 residues enable easy synthesis, good predicted solubility, low immunogenicity risk, and potential for further optimization (e.g., stapling for stability or conjugation for targeted delivery). Physicochemical profiles are favorable, but these remain hypothetical, necessitating preclinical testing in FLCN-deficient models for efficacy, specificity, and safety without exacerbating pathway imbalances.
