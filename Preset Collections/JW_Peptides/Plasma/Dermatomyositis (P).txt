Dermatomyositis is a rare autoimmune inflammatory myopathy characterized by progressive proximal muscle weakness and distinctive cutaneous manifestations, often accompanied by systemic involvement. It primarily affects adults between 40 and 60 years old and children, with a female predominance. The exact causes remain unclear, but a combination of genetic susceptibility (e.g., associations with HLA-DR3 and HLA-B8 alleles), environmental triggers such as viral infections (e.g., coxsackievirus, retroviruses), ultraviolet radiation exposure, and potential malignancy associations (paraneoplastic in 15-25% of adult cases) contribute to disease onset. Symptoms include symmetric proximal muscle weakness leading to difficulty rising from chairs, climbing stairs, or combing hair; fatigue; dysphagia due to pharyngeal muscle involvement; and skin changes like heliotrope rash (violaceous periorbital edema), Gottron's papules (erythematous plaques over knuckles), shawl sign (erythema over shoulders and upper back), mechanic's hands (hyperkeratotic fissured skin on palms), and calcinosis cutis (subcutaneous calcium deposits, more common in juvenile forms). Systemic complications encompass interstitial lung disease (up to 40% of cases, often rapidly progressive in anti-MDA5 antibody-positive patients), cardiac involvement (arrhythmias, myocarditis), gastrointestinal dysmotility, and increased malignancy risk (e.g., ovarian, lung, gastrointestinal cancers). The skin-muscle axis highlights shared inflammatory mechanisms, while heterogeneity in autoantibody profiles defines clinical subsets (e.g., anti-MDA5 linked to amyopathic form and lung disease).
