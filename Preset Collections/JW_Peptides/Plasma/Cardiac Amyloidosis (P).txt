Cardiac amyloidosis is a restrictive infiltrative cardiomyopathy caused by extracellular deposition of misfolded protein fibrils in the myocardium, leading to thickened ventricular walls, diastolic dysfunction, impaired relaxation, atrial enlargement, arrhythmias, conduction disturbances, and progressive heart failure. Symptoms include exertional dyspnea, peripheral edema, fatigue, orthostatic hypotension, and syncope, with poor prognosis if untreated due to high mortality from heart failure or sudden death. The two primary forms are immunoglobulin light-chain (AL) amyloidosis, stemming from plasma cell dyscrasia producing excess monoclonal light chains that misfold, and transthyretin (ATTR) amyloidosis, arising from dissociation of the transthyretin tetramer into monomers that aggregate, either in wild-type (age-related) or variant (hereditary) forms. Pathophysiology involves proteotoxic stress from soluble oligomers, mechanical disruption of tissue architecture by insoluble fibrils, oxidative stress, inflammation, endothelial dysfunction, and direct cytotoxicity to cardiomyocytes.
