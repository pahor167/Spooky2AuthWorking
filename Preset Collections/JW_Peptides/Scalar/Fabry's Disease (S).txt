Fabry's Disease, also known as Fabry disease, is a rare X-linked lysosomal storage disorder caused by mutations in the GLA gene, which encodes the enzyme alpha-galactosidase A ({alpha}-Gal A). This genetic defect leads to deficient or absent enzyme activity, resulting in the progressive accumulation of glycosphingolipids, particularly globotriaosylceramide (Gb3) and its deacylated derivative lyso-Gb3, in the lysosomes of various cell types throughout the body. The accumulation begins in utero and continues throughout life, affecting multiple organs and systems. Early symptoms often appear in childhood or adolescence and include acroparesthesia (intense burning pain in the hands and feet triggered by stress, temperature changes, or exercise), angiokeratomas (small, dark red spots on the skin due to vascular dilation), hypohidrosis or anhidrosis (reduced or absent sweating leading to heat intolerance), gastrointestinal disturbances such as abdominal pain, diarrhea, and nausea, and corneal opacities known as cornea verticillata. As the disease progresses into adulthood, it causes severe complications including chronic kidney disease leading to end-stage renal failure, cardiac manifestations such as left ventricular hypertrophy, arrhythmias, valvular dysfunction, and ischemic heart disease, cerebrovascular events like transient ischemic attacks and strokes, and neurological issues including hearing loss, tinnitus, and peripheral neuropathy. The pathophysiology involves not only direct lysosomal dysfunction and substrate accumulation but also secondary processes such as chronic inflammation mediated by innate immune responses, oxidative stress from reactive oxygen species, mitochondrial dysfunction, endothelial cell injury leading to vascular pathology, autophagy impairment, ferroptosis in renal podocytes, and fibrosis in organs like the heart and kidneys. These cascading effects amplify organ damage and contribute to reduced life expectancy, with males typically experiencing more severe symptoms due to the X-linked inheritance, while females can have variable expression due to X-chromosome inactivation.
