Drusen bodies are extracellular deposits composed primarily of lipids, proteins, amyloid-beta, complement components, and other debris that accumulate between the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) and Bruch's membrane in the macula of the eye. These deposits are a hallmark of early and intermediate stages of age-related macular degeneration (AMD), a leading cause of vision loss in individuals over 50 years old. The formation of drusen is driven by multiple factors, including aging, genetic predisposition (such as variants in complement factor H and other complement genes), chronic inflammation, oxidative stress from reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated by high metabolic activity in the retina, lipid metabolism dysregulation, and impaired clearance mechanisms by the RPE. Small, hard drusen are common with aging and may not cause symptoms, but larger, soft drusen or numerous drusen increase the risk of progression to advanced AMD. Symptoms typically emerge as drusen grow and disrupt RPE function, leading to blurred or distorted central vision (metamorphopsia), dark spots (scotomas), reduced contrast sensitivity, and difficulty with tasks requiring fine visual acuity like reading or driving. In pathophysiology, drusen contribute to RPE atrophy, photoreceptor degeneration, and can trigger the transition to geographic atrophy (advanced dry AMD) or choroidal neovascularization (wet AMD). Oxidative stress damages mitochondrial function in RPE cells, inflammation activates the complement cascade leading to membrane attack complex formation and cell lysis, and immune cell infiltration exacerbates tissue damage, creating a vicious cycle of deposition and degeneration.
