Myokymia is a neurological disorder manifesting as involuntary, fine, continuous, undulating muscle fasciculations or twitching, often described as worm-like rippling under the skin. It most commonly affects the eyelids (eyelid myokymia), but can involve facial muscles, limbs, or rarely other sites such as the superior oblique muscle (superior oblique myokymia). Benign forms, particularly eyelid myokymia, are common, transient, and triggered by stress, fatigue, caffeine, or electrolyte imbalances, typically resolving spontaneously. Pathological myokymia arises from peripheral nerve hyperexcitability due to disrupted ion channel function, demyelination (e.g., in multiple sclerosis or post-radiation), autoimmune attack on voltage-gated potassium channel complexes (often involving antibodies to LGI1 or CASPR2), genetic channelopathies (e.g., KCNA1 mutations in episodic ataxia type 1), or brainstem lesions. Pathophysiologically, reduced potassium efflux prolongs neuronal depolarization, leading to spontaneous or ephaptic firing, while autoimmune or demyelinating processes exacerbate membrane instability and ectopic discharge generation.
