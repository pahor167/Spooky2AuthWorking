Alkaptonuria (AKU) is a rare autosomal recessive genetic disorder caused by mutations in the HGD gene on chromosome 3q13.33, leading to a deficiency in the enzyme homogentisate 1,2-dioxygenase (HGD), which is crucial in the catabolic pathway of phenylalanine and tyrosine. This deficiency results in the accumulation of homogentisic acid (HGA), a metabolite that, when oxidized, forms a dark polymeric pigment that deposits in connective tissues, a phenomenon known as ochronosis. The pigment deposition primarily affects cartilage, tendons, ligaments, heart valves, kidneys, prostate, and skin, causing progressive tissue damage. Symptoms often emerge in the third or fourth decade of life and include darkening of urine upon exposure to air (due to HGA oxidation), blue-black pigmentation of the ears (ochronotic pigmentation), sclera, and skin, chronic joint pain and stiffness particularly in the spine and large joints (ochronotic arthropathy), spinal deformities such as kyphosis or scoliosis, heart valve thickening and stenosis (especially aortic valve), kidney stones, prostate stones, and occasionally respiratory issues from spinal involvement. The pathophysiology involves multiple mechanisms: HGA-induced oxidative stress leading to cell damage and apoptosis, chronic inflammation triggered by pigment deposition, secondary amyloidosis through serum amyloid A (SAA) aggregation and fibril formation, enhanced osteoclast activity causing bone resorption and joint destruction, and altered extracellular matrix integrity. Without treatment, the disease progresses to severe disability, requiring joint replacements and cardiovascular interventions. Current management includes symptomatic relief with painkillers, physiotherapy, and surgery, while nitisinone (a small molecule inhibitor of upstream enzyme HPPD) reduces HGA levels but causes tyrosinemia, necessitating a low-phenylalanine/tyrosine diet.
