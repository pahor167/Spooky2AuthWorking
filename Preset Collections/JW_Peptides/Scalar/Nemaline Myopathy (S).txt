Nemaline Myopathy (NM) is a genetically heterogeneous congenital myopathy characterized by muscle weakness, hypotonia, and the presence of nemaline rods-thread-like structures composed of Z-disk proteins-in muscle fibers, leading to impaired sarcomere function and contractility. It arises primarily from mutations in genes encoding thin filament proteins, such as NEB (nebulin), ACTA1 (alpha-actin), TPM3 (tropomyosin 3), and others like TNNT1, CFL2, and LMOD3, disrupting actin filament assembly, stability, and regulation. Environmental factors may exacerbate symptoms, but the core pathophysiology involves defective thin filament length control, altered cross-bridge cycling kinetics, increased protein degradation, and mitochondrial dysfunction, resulting in early-onset weakness (often neonatal or infantile), respiratory insufficiency, bulbar dysfunction, delayed motor milestones, and in severe cases, early mortality. Relapsing or adult-onset forms, like sporadic late-onset nemaline myopathy (SLONM), may involve immune-mediated mechanisms with monoclonal gammopathy. Progression varies by subtype, with cumulative disability from recurrent respiratory infections, scoliosis, and joint contractures, distinguishing it from other congenital myopathies by its rod pathology and genetic diversity.
