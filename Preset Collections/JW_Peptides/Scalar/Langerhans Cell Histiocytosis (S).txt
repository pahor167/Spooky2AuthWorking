Langerhans Cell Histiocytosis (LCH) is a rare, heterogeneous disorder characterized by the clonal proliferation of Langerhans-like cells, which are specialized dendritic cells derived from myeloid precursors, leading to granulomatous lesions in various organs. It spans a spectrum from localized, self-limiting lesions to multisystem, life-threatening disease, with an incidence of 1-2 per million in adults and 5-9 per million in children. Genetic mutations, particularly BRAF V600E in the MAPK/ERK pathway (present in ~60% of cases), drive uncontrolled cell proliferation, while environmental triggers like smoking may contribute to pulmonary LCH. Symptoms depend on organ involvement: bone lesions cause pain and fractures; skin presents as rashes or ulcers; pulmonary involvement leads to cough, dyspnea, and pneumothorax; central nervous system (CNS) lesions result in diabetes insipidus, ataxia, or neurodegeneration; multisystem disease includes fever, hepatosplenomegaly, cytopenias, and lymphadenopathy. Without treatment, severe cases progress to organ failure, with 5-year survival ~30% in high-risk multisystem LCH; management historically relies on chemotherapy, but targeted therapies are emerging.
