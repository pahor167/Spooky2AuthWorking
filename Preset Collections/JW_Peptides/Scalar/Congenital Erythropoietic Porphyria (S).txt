Congenital erythropoietic porphyria (CEP), also known as Günther disease, is an extremely rare autosomal recessive disorder caused by severely deficient activity of uroporphyrinogen III synthase (UROS), the fourth enzyme in the heme biosynthetic pathway. This deficiency leads to massive accumulation of non-physiologic type I isomers (uroporphyrin I and coproporphyrin I) in erythroid precursors, red blood cells, plasma, and tissues. Clinical manifestations typically begin in infancy with dark red urine staining diapers, followed by severe cutaneous photosensitivity upon light exposure, resulting in blistering, erosions, infections, scarring, and progressive photomutilation of sun-exposed areas (face, hands). Additional features include hemolytic anemia (often transfusion-dependent in severe cases), erythrodontia (reddish teeth fluorescing under UV light), hypertrichosis, splenomegaly, osteopenia, and in severe untreated cases, significant morbidity from deformities and infections. The disease severity varies with residual UROS activity; milder late-onset forms exist but are uncommon.
