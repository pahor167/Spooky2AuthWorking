Griscelli syndrome (GS) is a rare autosomal recessive disorder characterized by partial cutaneous albinism manifesting as silvery-gray hair and hypopigmented skin due to abnormal melanosome distribution and accumulation in melanocytes. Three subtypes exist based on genetic defects in the tripartite protein complex governing actin-dependent intracellular vesicular transport: type 1 (GS1, MYO5A mutations) features primary severe neurological impairment including intellectual disability, seizures, and progressive neurodegeneration without significant immunodeficiency; type 2 (GS2, RAB27A mutations) combines hypopigmentation with life-threatening immunodeficiency characterized by defective cytotoxic T-lymphocyte and natural killer cell granule exocytosis, leading to recurrent hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH) with hypercytokinemia, fever, organomegaly, cytopenias, and high mortality if untreated; type 3 (GS3, MLPH mutations) is restricted to pigmentary dilution without neurological or immunological involvement. The core pathophysiology across subtypes involves disruption of RAB27A-MLPH-MYO5A interactions essential for capturing melanosomes and lytic granules on myosin Va motors for peripheral transport along actin filaments, resulting in central clumping, impaired melanin transfer to keratinocytes, defective immune synapse formation, uncontrolled macrophage/T-cell activation in HLH, and axonal transport deficits in neurons.

Current management lacks targeted pharmacotherapy; GS3 requires none, GS1 is supportive for neurological sequelae, while GS2 demands urgent HLH control with immunochemotherapy (e.g., corticosteroids, cyclosporine, etoposide) followed by allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation as the only curative option. No established polypeptide therapeutics exist. The proposed polypeptides are all designed conceptual short C-terminally amidated amphipathic cationic peptides leveraging principles of cell-penetrating and membrane-modulating peptides for feasible synthesis, proteolytic resistance, and intracellular delivery. These designs employ alternating hydrophobic/aromatic and cationic residues to form alpha-helical structures with distinct amphipathic faces, enabling potential correction of trafficking defects through enhanced vesicle-motor interactions, granule docking facilitation, cytokine storm mitigation via immune modulation, and neuroprotection. The first designed polypeptide uses phenylalanine-lysine alternation for balanced hydrophobicity and charge to support melanosome/granule peripheral transport; the second incorporates tryptophan-arginine for superior membrane insertion and exocytosis promotion; the third features tyrosine-lysine for phenolic antioxidant properties alongside trafficking aid; the fourth employs leucine-arginine for robust helical stabilization of actin-based motors. Collectively, these complementary designs target multifaceted restoration of vesicular dynamics and immune homeostasis.
