This mutation leads to the production of mutant huntingtin (mHTT), which misfolds, aggregates, and triggers a cascade of pathological events. Symptoms usually emerge in mid-adulthood, around ages 30-50, and include involuntary choreiform movements, psychiatric disturbances such as depression, anxiety, and irritability, cognitive decline progressing to dementia, and motor impairments like bradykinesia, rigidity, and gait instability in later stages. Pathophysiology involves neuronal loss primarily in the striatum (caudate and putamen) and cerebral cortex, with mHTT aggregates forming intranuclear inclusions that disrupt cellular functions. Key mechanisms include transcriptional dysregulation, impaired protein degradation via autophagy and the ubiquitin-proteasome system, mitochondrial dysfunction leading to energy deficits and oxidative stress, excitotoxicity from glutamate overload, neuroinflammation with activated microglia and elevated cytokines, defective axonal transport, apoptosis, metabolic and hormonal imbalances, and alterations in the gut-brain axis via microbiota dysbiosis. Systemic effects extend beyond the brain, affecting peripheral tissues like muscles, adipose, and the gastrointestinal system, contributing to weight loss, metabolic disturbances, and increased susceptibility to infections. Disease progression is relentless, leading to severe disability and death within 15-20 years of symptom onset, with no curative treatments currently available, only symptomatic management.
