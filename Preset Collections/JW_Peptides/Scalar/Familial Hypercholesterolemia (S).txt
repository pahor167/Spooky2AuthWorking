Familial hypercholesterolemia (FH) is an inherited genetic disorder characterized by significantly elevated levels of low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C) in the blood, leading to premature cardiovascular disease. It is primarily caused by mutations in genes involved in LDL metabolism, most commonly the LDL receptor (LDLR) gene (accounting for about 90% of cases), but also in APOB (encoding apolipoprotein B) and PCSK9 (encoding proprotein convertase subtilisin/kexin type 9). These mutations are typically autosomal dominant, with heterozygous FH affecting approximately 1 in 250 individuals and the rarer homozygous form occurring in about 1 in 300,000. Symptoms often include visible signs such as tendon xanthomas (cholesterol deposits in tendons, especially Achilles), xanthelasmas (around eyes), and corneal arcus (white ring around the iris), but many patients are asymptomatic until cardiovascular events occur. Pathophysiology involves impaired clearance of LDL particles from circulation: LDLR mutations reduce receptor number or function on hepatocytes, APOB mutations impair LDL binding to receptors, and PCSK9 gain-of-function mutations accelerate LDLR degradation. This results in lifelong high LDL-C exposure, promoting atherosclerosis through endothelial dysfunction, foam cell formation, plaque buildup in arteries, and increased risk of coronary artery disease, myocardial infarction, and stroke, often manifesting in the 40s-50s for heterozygous and childhood for homozygous FH.
